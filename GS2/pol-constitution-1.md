### Q1. Constitution of India is a living document, how ?

- **Intro:**
  - A "Living Document" is one that evolves with the changing needs of society rather than remaining static. The Indian Constitution achieves this through a balance of "Rigidity and Flexibility," guided by **Article 368**, judicial interpretations, and democratic conventions.

- **Body:**
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Amendment Power (Art 368)}}}$, **Legislative Agility**: India has passed over 100 amendments in 75 years, adapting to changes like the GST (101st) or EWS reservation (103rd).
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Basic Structure Doctrine}}}$: The SC in *Kesavananda Bharati (1973)* ensured the document lives without losing its soul by preventing the destruction of its core values.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Expansion of Fundamental Rights}}}$: Through judicial activism, **Article 21** has expanded to include the right to privacy, clean environment, and education.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Navtej Singh Johar v. UoI (2018)}}}$: The SC held that the Constitution must be a "Transformative" document that evolves to protect the rights of the LGBTQ+ community.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Accommodation of Diversity}}}$: Schedules V and VI allow for unique administrative arrangements in tribal areas, reflecting a living response to regional aspirations.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{73rd & 74th Amendments}}}$: The addition of Parts IX and IXA successfully "lived" the dream of local self-governance which was initially just a DPSP (Art 40).
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Doctrine of Living Tree}}}$: The judiciary treats the Constitution as an organic growth that must be interpreted in light of modern realities, not 1950s intentions.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Fundamental Duties (42nd Amd)}}}$, **Citizen Responsibility**: The document grew to include the duties of citizens, reflecting an evolving social contract.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Evolving Federalism}}}$: The creation of the GST Council shows the document's ability to innovate new federal structures for economic unity.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Puttaswamy Judgement (2017)}}}$: Recognized the "Right to Privacy" as a fundamental right, showing the Constitution's ability to respond to the digital age.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Secularism & Socialism}}}$: These terms were formally added to the Preamble in 1976, solidifying the document's commitment to evolving social goals.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{106th Amendment (2023)}}}$: The recent move for Women’s Reservation in legislatures shows the document adapting to modern demands for gender justice.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Internal Emergency Changes}}}$: Post-44th Amendment, the removal of "Internal Disturbance" for "Armed Rebellion" showed the document learning from history.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Right to Education}}}$: The insertion of **Article 21A** reflects the Constitution responding to the national need for universal literacy.

- **Conclusion:**
  - The Indian Constitution is not a "finished product" but a "continuing process." Its beauty lies in its ability to bend without breaking, ensuring it remains as relevant to a digital India as it was to a newly independent one.

### Q2. Discuss Section 66A of IT Act, with reference to its alleged violation of Article 19 of the Constitution.

**Intro:**
Section 66A of the IT Act criminalized "offensive" online messages but was struck down for violating the fundamental right to free speech under Article 19(1)(a).

**Body:**
*   **Constitutional & Legal Dimensions:**
    *   **Subjective Vagueness:** The law used undefined terms like "annoyance," allowing arbitrary police action (e.g., the 2012 Palghar arrests for a Facebook post).
    *   **Doctrine of Overbreadth:** It lacked a nexus with Art 19(2) restrictions, as causing "annoyance" is not a valid ground for restricting speech.
    *   **Chilling Effect:** The threat of a 3-year jail term led to widespread self-censorship, stifling political dissent and online satire.
    *   **Shreya Singhal v. UoI (2015):** The Supreme Court struck it down for being "vague, arbitrary, and disproportionate," failing to distinguish discussion from incitement.
    *   **Shreya Singhal v. UoI (2015):** The SC struck it down for being "vague and disproportionate," failing to distinguish between mere advocacy and incitement.
    *   **Violation of Art 19(2):** It criminalized speech that didn't threaten public order or security, exceeding the "reasonable restrictions" allowed by the Constitution.
    *   **Right to Receive Information:** By blocking "offensive" content, it indirectly violated the citizens' right to access diverse viewpoints.
*   **Legal & Procedural Dimensions:**
    *   **Lack of Mens Rea:** The section didn't require a specific "criminal intent," leading to the prosecution of innocent shared content.
    *   **Equality under Art 14:** It created a discriminatory regime where online speech was punished more harshly than the same speech in physical media.
    *   **Procedural Safeguards:** Unlike other sections of the IT Act, 66A lacked built-in safeguards, giving lower-level officers unchecked power to arrest.
    *   **International Law Alignment:** The SC noted that such vague laws violate the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) standards.
*   **Democratic & Social Dimensions:**
    *   **Political Dissent:** It was frequently misused by state actors to silence critics and whistleblowers (e.g., the Aseem Trivedi cartoon case).
    *   **Rule of Law:** A law that fails to tell a citizen what is forbidden is "void for vagueness," undermining the predictability of the legal system.
*   **Way Forward:**
    *   **Judicial Training:** Ensure law enforcement is updated on "zombie laws" to prevent continued use of struck-down sections in FIRs.
    *   **Specific Legislation:** New regulations should focus on "incitement to violence" rather than "offensive" content to safeguard democratic discourse.
    *   **Narrowly Tailored Laws:** Any future regulation must use precise definitions for "hate speech" or "incitement" to avoid encroaching on free expression.

**Conclusion:**
The removal of Section 66A ensures digital expression is protected from arbitrary state overreach, upholding the core values of the Indian Constitution.

### Q3.Recent directives from Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas are perceived by the ‘Nagas’ as a threat to override the exceptional status enjoyed by the State. Discuss in light of Article 371A of the Indian Constitution.

**Intro:**
Article 371A provides Nagaland special status over its land and resources, creating a constitutional conflict with Central directives on petroleum exploration.

**Body:**
*   **Federal & Socio-Economic Dimensions:**
    *   **Constitutional Veto:** Art 371A(1)(a)(iv) mandates that no Central law on land/resources applies to Nagaland without a resolution from the State Assembly.
    *   **Customary Law Conflict:** Naga customary laws govern community ownership, which clashes with Central acts like the Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board Act.
    *   **Economic Autonomy:** Local bodies perceive Central licensing as a threat to their share of royalties and rights over sub-surface minerals.
    *   **Economic Autonomy:** Local bodies perceive Central licensing as a threat to their share of royalties and community rights over sub-surface minerals.
    *   **Sub-surface Rights:** Unlike the rest of India where the Union owns minerals, Art 371A implies that Naga people own the resources below their land.
    *   **Social Trust:** Unilateral directives are seen as a dilution of the "16-Point Agreement" (1960) that led to Nagaland's formation.
*   **Legal & Administrative Dimensions:**
    *   **Non-Obstante Clause:** The article starts with "Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution," giving it supremacy over General Union list powers.
    *   **State Legislative Competence:** The Nagaland Assembly passed its own PNG Regulations (2012), asserting its sole right to regulate oil and gas.
    *   **Conflict of Interest:** Directives often ignore the "Village Councils," which are the primary constitutional authorities for land management in Nagaland.
    *   **Legal Precedence:** High Court rulings have previously emphasized that the Union cannot bypass the Assembly's resolution requirement for resource extraction.
*   **Security & Developmental Dimensions:**
    *   **Peace Process Impact:** Perceived violations of Art 371A can jeopardize the ongoing Indo-Naga peace talks and create civil unrest.
    *   **Environmental Concerns:** Local tribes fear that Central directives might prioritize extraction speed over the fragile ecological balance of tribal lands.
    *   **Resource Federalism:** The dispute highlights the need for a "special fiscal model" where Nagaland retains a higher percentage of resource revenue.
*   **Way Forward:**
    *   **Cooperative Federalism:** Establish a joint consultative committee involving the Ministry and Naga Tribal Councils to align national interests with local rights.
    *   **Revenue Sharing:** Develop transparent models that prioritize the developmental needs of the specific Naga communities where resources are extracted.
    *   **Joint Management Models:** Establish tripartite committees (Centre-State-Tribes) to co-manage exploration while respecting customary ownership.
    *   **Institutional Clarity:** The Supreme Court could provide a definitive interpretation of "land and its resources" to prevent recurring friction between MoPNG and the State.

**Conclusion:**
Respecting Nagaland's "exceptional status" is vital for regional stability and maintaining the integrity of the federal contract under Article 371A.
