### Account for the reduced frequency of use of article 356 post 1990s.

- **Intro:**
  - **Article 356** (President's Rule) provides for the takeover of state administration by the Centre in case of a "failure of constitutional machinery." Once called a "Dead Letter" by Ambedkar, its frequent misuse led to a major shift post-1990s towards a more restrained application.

- **Body:**
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)}}}$, **The Game Changer**: The SC made the proclamation under Art 356 subject to **Judicial Review**, effectively ending its arbitrary use.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Floor Test Requirement}}}$: Following the *Bommai* guidelines, the majority of a government must be tested on the floor of the House, not in the Governor's bungalow.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rise of Coalition Politics}}}$: Post-1990, the era of single-party dominance ended; national parties needed regional allies, making it politically suicidal to dismiss state governments.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Active Judiciary}}}$: Recent interventions in Uttarakhand (2016) and Arunachal Pradesh (2016) saw the SC quashing President's Rule, acting as a strong deterrent for the Centre.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Role of the President}}}$: Presidents like K.R. Narayanan set precedents by returning cabinet recommendations for Art 356 for reconsideration (e.g., in Bihar and UP).
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Sarkaria Commission}}}$: Its recommendation that Art 356 should be used as a "measure of last resort" gradually gained institutional acceptance.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Punchhi Commission}}}$: Further reinforced that localized disturbances should be handled without dismissing the entire state government.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Inter-State Council}}}$: The activation of Art 263 provided a platform for dialogue, reducing the "conflict-first" approach between the Centre and States.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Media and Public Scrutiny}}}$: The 24/7 news cycle and social media have made the "undemocratic" dismissal of state governments a high-cost political move.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Basic Structure Doctrine}}}$: Federalism being a part of the basic structure ensures that any attempt to dilute it via Art 356 is met with strict judicial scrutiny.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Federal Maturity}}}$: Political parties have increasingly moved towards "Cooperative Federalism" where economic development takes priority over political vendetta.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rameshwar Prasad Case (2006)}}}$, **Governor's Role**: The SC held that the Governor cannot recommend Art 356 just to prevent a particular party from forming a government.
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Impact of Art 355}}}$: The Centre now focuses more on its duty under Art 355 to *protect* states against internal disturbance rather than using it as a pretext for dismissal.

- **Conclusion:**
  - The journey of Article 356 from a "political weapon" to a "last resort" signifies the maturation of Indian Federalism. The *SR Bommai* verdict remains the strongest shield for state autonomy against central overreach.
