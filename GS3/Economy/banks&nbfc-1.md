### Q1. How well RBI has performed the job of central regulator of banks.

**BODY:**

2.  **Inflation Targeting (Success):** Adopted an inflation-targeting framework, keeping inflation within the mandated band for the most part; Example: The Monetary Policy Committee's actions have helped anchor inflationary expectations.
3.  **Promoting Digital Payments (Success):** Spearheaded the digital payments revolution through initiatives like UPI; Example: UPI has become a global benchmark for real-time digital payments, fostering a less-cash economy.

5.  **Regulatory Lapses (Failure):** Failed to pre-empt major frauds and collapses in the financial sector; Example: The PMC Bank crisis and the collapse of Yes Bank exposed significant gaps in regulatory supervision.

6.  **Cybersecurity Challenges (Emerging):** Struggling to keep pace with evolving cyber threats in the digital banking landscape.
7.  **Credit Growth Management (Mixed):** Balancing the need for credit growth with asset quality concerns has been a continuous challenge.
8.  **Financial Inclusion (Success):** Successfully pushed for Jan Dhan accounts and digital payments, bringing millions into the formal financial system.
9.  **Supervisory Convergence (Ongoing):** Efforts to harmonize supervision across different financial entities (banks, NBFCs) are still evolving.
10. **Monetary Policy Transmission (Challenges):** Ensuring that policy rate changes effectively translate into lending rates for the real economy.

### Q8. What are the challenges of NPA in India ?

**BODY:**

1.  **Erodes Bank Profitability:** Banks have to make provisions for bad loans, which reduces their profits and capital base.

4.  **Moral Hazard:** The failure to resolve NPAs in a timely manner can create a moral hazard, where borrowers feel they can default without consequences.
5.  **Fiscal Burden on Government:** The government, as the majority owner of PSBs, has to infuse capital repeatedly to keep them afloat, straining public finances.
6.  **Credit Crunch:** Banks become risk-averse, leading to a reduction in fresh lending, impacting economic growth.
7.  **Reduced Profitability:** Higher provisioning requirements for NPAs directly hit the bank's bottom line.
8.  **Loss of Depositor Confidence:** Persistent NPA issues can erode public trust in the banking system.
9.  **Increased Cost of Borrowing:** Banks with high NPAs may have to pay more to raise funds, which is passed on to borrowers.
10. **Twin Balance Sheet Problem:** Contributes to the broader issue of stressed corporate balance sheets and bank NPAs, creating a vicious cycle.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, a robust and time-bound mechanism for NPA resolution is critical for cleaning up bank balance sheets and reviving the credit cycle.

### Q12. What are the contributions of IBC code in solving NPA problem in India ?

**INTRO:** The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC), 2016, is a landmark reform aimed at creating a time-bound and market-linked resolution process for stressed assets.

**BODY:**

1.  **Time-Bound Resolution:** The Code mandates a strict timeline (initially 180 days, extendable to 330 days) for resolving insolvency, reducing delays.
2.  **Shift in Power Dynamics:** It shifts the control of the defaulting company from the promoters to a committee of creditors, empowering lenders.
3.  **Improved Recovery Rates:** The recovery for creditors under IBC has been significantly higher compared to previous mechanisms like SARFAESI and Debt Recovery Tribunals.
4.  **Behavioral Change in Promoters:** The fear of losing their company has instilled credit discipline among promoters and reduced willful defaults.
5.  **Market-Determined Process:** It facilitates a transparent, market-driven process for resolving stress, leading to better value maximization for assets.
6.  **Consolidation of Laws:** Replaced a fragmented legal framework with a unified, comprehensive law for insolvency and bankruptcy.
7.  **Value Maximization:** Focuses on maximizing the value of assets of the corporate debtor, benefiting all stakeholders.
8.  **Reduced Information Asymmetry:** Promotes transparency by requiring disclosure of financial information during the process.
9.  **Ease of Doing Business:** Improved India's ranking in the World Bank's Ease of Doing Business report, particularly in the 'resolving insolvency' parameter.
10. **Protection for MSMEs:** Specific provisions for fast-track insolvency resolution for MSMEs, recognizing their unique needs.

**CONCLUSION:** While facing challenges, the IBC has fundamentally changed the credit culture and provided a robust framework for tackling the NPA problem in India.

### Q13. What are the limitations of IBC code ?

**INTRO:** The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC), 2016, is a landmark reform aimed at creating a time-bound and market-linked resolution process for stressed assets.

**BODY:**

1.  **Delays in Resolution:** Despite the mandated timelines, a large number of cases are exceeding the 330-day limit due to litigation and procedural delays.
2.  **Overburdened NCLT:** The National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) benches are overburdened with cases, leading to a backlog and slowing down the process.
3.  **Low Recovery in Liquidation:** In cases where resolution fails and the company goes into liquidation, the recovery rates have been very low.
4.  **Haircuts for Lenders:** In many resolutions, lenders, especially public sector banks, have had to take significant haircuts (losses) on their loans.
5.  **Limited Success in Real Estate:** The IBC has faced challenges in resolving insolvency in the real estate sector due to the complexities involving multiple homebuyers.
6.  **Overburdened NCLAT:** The National Company Law Appellate Tribunal (NCLAT) also faces a significant backlog, leading to further delays in appeals.
7.  **Valuation Challenges:** Difficulty in accurately valuing assets, especially for complex or specialized industries.
8.  **Lack of Specialized Resolution Professionals:** Shortage of experienced and qualified Insolvency Resolution Professionals (IRPs).
9.  **Inter-Creditor Disputes:** Conflicts among different classes of creditors (e.g., secured vs. unsecured) can prolong the resolution process.
10. **Impact on Going Concern:** The long resolution process can sometimes lead to further deterioration of the company's value, making it harder to revive.

**CONCLUSION:** While facing challenges, the IBC has fundamentally changed the credit culture and provided a robust framework for tackling the NPA problem in India.

### Q18. Why are shadow banks considered to be a threat to the Indian economy ?

**INTRO:** Shadow banks, or Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs), are financial intermediaries that provide credit but are not subject to the same stringent regulations as traditional banks.

**BODY:**

1.  **Systemic Risk:** The interconnectedness between NBFCs and commercial banks means that the failure of a large NBFC can trigger a contagion effect across the entire financial system; Example: The IL&FS crisis of 2018.
2.  **Regulatory Arbitrage:** They operate with less stringent regulations regarding capital adequacy and liquidity, allowing them to take on higher risks.
3.  **Asset-Liability Mismatch:** Many NBFCs borrow short-term funds from the market to finance long-term loans, creating a significant asset-liability mismatch that makes them vulnerable to liquidity shocks.
4.  **Lack of Safety Net:** Unlike commercial banks, NBFC deposits are not covered by deposit insurance, exposing depositors to higher risk.
5.  **Pro-Cyclical Nature:** They tend to expand lending rapidly during economic booms and contract sharply during downturns, amplifying business cycles and economic volatility.
6.  **Contagion Risk:** The failure of one large NBFC can trigger a liquidity crisis across the financial system, as seen with IL&FS.
7.  **Opaque Operations:** Some NBFCs have complex ownership structures and less transparent operations, making supervision difficult.
8.  **Over-Leveraging:** Aggressive lending practices and high leverage can increase their vulnerability to defaults.
9.  **Credit Quality Concerns:** Lending to riskier segments or sectors can lead to a deterioration of their asset quality.
10. **Interconnectedness with Banks:** Significant borrowing by NBFCs from banks creates a direct channel for stress transmission to the banking system.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, strengthening the regulatory and supervisory framework for large, systemically important NBFCs is crucial to mitigate the risks they pose to financial stability.

### Q19. Discuss the reforms undertaken by the authorities to address shadow banks challenge.

**INTRO:** Shadow banks, or Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs), are financial intermediaries that provide credit but are not subject to the same stringent regulations as traditional banks.

**BODY:**

1.  **Scale-Based Regulation:** The RBI introduced a four-layered regulatory framework (Base, Middle, Upper, and Top Layers) for NBFCs based on their size and systemic importance.
2.  **Prompt Corrective Action (PCA) Framework:** The PCA framework, similar to that for banks, has been extended to large NBFCs to enable timely supervisory intervention.
3.  **Enhanced Capital Requirements:** The RBI has harmonized capital adequacy norms for large NBFCs with those of commercial banks, requiring them to maintain higher capital buffers.
4.  **Liquidity Risk Management:** Introduction of the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) for large NBFCs to ensure they hold sufficient high-quality liquid assets to survive a stress scenario.
5.  **Strengthened Corporate Governance:** The RBI has issued guidelines to improve governance standards, including the appointment of a Chief Risk Officer and strengthening the role of the board.
6.  **Prompt Corrective Action (PCA) Framework:** Extended the PCA framework to large NBFCs to enable timely supervisory intervention based on financial thresholds.
7.  **Core Investment Companies (CICs) Regulation:** Stricter oversight of CICs to prevent concentration risk and ensure financial stability within groups.
8.  **Online Loan App Regulation:** RBI has tightened regulations on digital lending apps, many of which are NBFCs, to curb predatory lending practices.
9.  **Increased Disclosure Norms:** Mandated greater transparency in financial reporting and asset quality for NBFCs.
10. **Harmonization of Regulations:** Efforts to bring NBFC regulations closer to those of banks, reducing regulatory arbitrage.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, strengthening the regulatory and supervisory framework for large, systemically important NBFCs is crucial to mitigate the risks they pose to financial stability.

### Q20. Justify the rationale behind Govt’s decision to setup a Bad Bank, despite the existence of IBC Code and SARFAESI Act.

**INTRO:** A 'Bad Bank', or the National Asset Reconstruction Company Ltd. (NARCL), has been set up to aggregate and consolidate stressed assets from commercial banks for resolution.

**BODY:**

1.  **Aggregation of Debt:** Existing mechanisms like IBC are creditor-specific. A bad bank can aggregate the entire debt of a single borrower from multiple banks, making resolution easier and faster.
2.  **Focused Resolution:** Commercial banks can focus on their core activity of lending, leaving the specialized and time-consuming task of resolving bad loans to a dedicated entity (the bad bank).
3.  **Overcoming Coordination Failures:** In cases with multiple lenders (consortium lending), it is often difficult to reach a consensus on a resolution plan. The bad bank overcomes this by becoming the single point of contact.
4.  **Faster Clean-up of Bank Balance Sheets:** It provides an immediate, one-time solution for banks to offload their legacy NPAs, freeing up capital for fresh lending.
5.  **Better Price Realization:** A specialized entity with expertise in asset resolution is likely to realize better value from the stressed assets compared to individual banks.
6.  **Market-Driven Resolution:** NARCL works with India Debt Resolution Company Ltd. (IDRCL) to facilitate a market-driven resolution process.
7.  **Reduces Moral Hazard for Banks:** Allows banks to focus on fresh lending without the burden of managing legacy bad loans.
8.  **Attracts Private Capital:** The IDRCL component is designed to attract private sector expertise and capital for asset resolution.
9.  **Systemic Stability:** By addressing large NPAs, it contributes to the overall stability of the banking system.
10. **Specialized Expertise:** A dedicated entity can develop specialized expertise in complex asset resolution, which individual banks may lack.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, the bad bank is not a replacement for IBC or SARFAESI but a complementary mechanism designed to address specific challenges in resolving large, legacy NPAs.

### Q22. What are Self Regulatory Bodies/Organisations (SRO).

**INTRO:** A Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO) is a non-governmental body that represents an industry or profession and is empowered to create and enforce its own rules and standards of conduct.

**BODY:**

1.  **Industry-Led Governance:** It is formed and led by industry participants to regulate their own members; Example: The Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI) for the mutual fund industry.
2.  **First-Line Regulator:** It acts as the first level of regulation, monitoring day-to-day conduct and reducing the direct supervisory burden on the main statutory regulator (e.g., SEBI, RBI).
3.  **Rule-Making Authority:** It sets ethical codes, best practices, and binding rules for its members to follow; Example: AMFI's code of conduct for mutual fund distributors.
4.  **Disciplinary Power:** It has the authority to investigate complaints and take disciplinary action, such as imposing fines or suspending membership, against non-compliant members.
5.  **Developmental Role:** It often works to develop the market, promote investor education, and act as a collective voice for the industry in policy matters.
6.  **Enhances Market Integrity:** By self-policing, SROs contribute to maintaining trust and confidence in the market.
7.  **Reduces Regulatory Burden:** They act as a first line of defense, reducing the direct supervisory load on statutory regulators.
8.  **Promotes Best Practices:** SROs encourage members to adopt high standards of conduct and operational efficiency.
9.  **Industry Representation:** They provide a unified voice for the industry in discussions with regulators and policymakers.
10. **Investor Protection:** By enforcing rules and providing grievance redressal, they indirectly protect investor interests.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, SROs play a crucial role in fostering market integrity and efficiency by combining industry expertise with regulatory oversight, complementing statutory bodies.

### Q23. Justify the rationale for setting up Self Regulatory Organisations ?

### Q25. What are the risks of artificial intelligence in financial intermediation?

**INTRO:** Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming financial intermediation by automating processes, enhancing risk management, and personalizing services for greater efficiency and inclusion.

**BODY:**

1.  **Algorithmic Bias:** AI models trained on historical data can perpetuate and even amplify existing biases, leading to discriminatory outcomes; Example: An AI loan model unfairly denying credit to certain demographic groups.
2.  **Lack of Transparency:** The "black box" nature of complex AI models makes it difficult to understand or explain their decision-making process, posing a challenge for accountability.
3.  **Cybersecurity Threats:** Centralized AI systems can become a prime target for sophisticated cyberattacks, potentially leading to large-scale data breaches or financial disruption.
4.  **Systemic Risk:** The widespread use of similar AI models by multiple financial institutions could lead to herd behavior and amplify market shocks, creating systemic risk.
5.  **Job Displacement:** Automation of routine tasks like data entry, customer service, and credit analysis could lead to significant job losses in the financial sector.

5.  **Data Privacy and Security:** AI systems require vast amounts of data, raising concerns about privacy breaches and the security of sensitive financial information.
6.  **Lack of Explainability (Black Box):** The complex nature of some AI models makes it difficult to understand how decisions are made, posing challenges for auditing and accountability.
7.  **Ethical Concerns:** Issues around fairness, transparency, and accountability in AI-driven financial decisions.
8.  **Regulatory Challenges:** Regulators struggle to keep pace with the rapid advancements in AI, leading to potential gaps in oversight.
9.  **Over-Reliance on AI:** Excessive dependence on AI without human oversight can lead to errors or system failures with significant financial consequences.
10. **Cost of Implementation:** Implementing and maintaining advanced AI systems can be very expensive, especially for smaller financial institutions.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, AI is fundamentally reshaping the financial landscape, but its adoption must be balanced with robust governance to manage associated risks like bias and security.
