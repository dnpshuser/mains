### Q1. How does this bad bank alleviate the pain of NPA?

**INTRO:** A 'Bad Bank', like the National Asset Reconstruction Company Ltd. (NARCL), alleviates the pain of Non-Performing Assets (NPAs) by consolidating stressed loans from banks for specialized resolution.

**BODY:**

1.  **Aggregation of Debt:** It consolidates a single borrower's debt from multiple banks into one entity; Example: This simplifies the resolution process by avoiding the need for coordination among many lenders in a consortium.
2.  **Focused Resolution:** Commercial banks can focus on their core lending activities, leaving the specialized and time-consuming task of resolving bad loans to the bad bank's expert team.
3.  **Faster Balance Sheet Cleanup:** It allows banks to offload their legacy NPAs in one go, immediately cleaning their balance sheets and freeing up capital for new lending.
4.  **Improved Bargaining Power:** As a single large creditor, the bad bank has greater bargaining power with the defaulting promoters during the resolution process.
5.  **Better Price Realization:** A dedicated entity with resolution expertise is better positioned to maximize the recovery value from the stressed assets compared to individual banks.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, the bad bank complements existing mechanisms like IBC by providing a focused and efficient approach to resolving the large, legacy NPAs in the banking system.

### Q2. What are Self Regulatory Bodies/Organisations (SRO).

**INTRO:** A Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO) is a non-governmental body that represents an industry or profession and is empowered to create and enforce its own rules and standards of conduct.

**BODY:**

1.  **Industry-Led Governance:** It is formed and led by industry participants to regulate their own members; Example: The Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI) for the mutual fund industry.
2.  **First-Line Regulator:** It acts as the first level of regulation, monitoring day-to-day conduct and reducing the direct supervisory burden on the main statutory regulator (e.g., SEBI, RBI).
3.  **Rule-Making Authority:** It sets ethical codes, best practices, and binding rules for its members to follow; Example: AMFI's code of conduct for mutual fund distributors.
4.  **Disciplinary Power:** It has the authority to investigate complaints and take disciplinary action, such as imposing fines or suspending membership, against non-compliant members.
5.  **Developmental Role:** It often works to develop the market, promote investor education, and act as a collective voice for the industry in policy matters.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, SROs play a crucial role in fostering market integrity and efficiency by combining industry expertise with regulatory oversight, complementing statutory bodies.

### Q3. Justify the rationale for setting up Self Regulatory Organisations ?

**INTRO:** A Self-Regulatory Organisation (SRO) is a non-governmental body that represents an industry or profession and is empowered to create and enforce its own rules and standards of conduct.

**BODY:**

1.  **Leveraging Industry Expertise:** SROs possess deep, specialized knowledge of their sector, enabling them to frame more nuanced and effective rules than a generalist statutory body.
2.  **Reducing Regulatory Burden:** They handle frontline supervision and dispute resolution, allowing the main regulator (like SEBI) to focus on broader systemic risks and policy-making.
3.  **Promoting Market Discipline:** Peer oversight and the risk of reputational damage encourage higher standards of conduct and voluntary compliance among members.
4.  **Speed and Flexibility:** SROs can adapt rules and standards more quickly to rapid market innovations and technological changes compared to the slower legislative process.
5.  **Cost-Effectiveness:** The operational costs of the SRO are borne by the industry members, reducing the financial burden on the government and taxpayers.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, SROs play a crucial role in fostering market integrity and efficiency by combining industry expertise with regulatory oversight, complementing statutory bodies.

### Q4. What are the applications of artificial intelligence in financial intermediation?

**INTRO:** Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming financial intermediation by automating processes, enhancing risk management, and personalizing services for greater efficiency and inclusion.

**BODY:**

1.  **Credit Scoring and Lending:** AI algorithms analyze vast datasets to assess creditworthiness more accurately, enabling loans for new-to-credit customers; Example: Fintech lenders using AI to approve small personal loans instantly.
2.  **Fraud Detection and Prevention:** Machine learning models identify unusual patterns in real-time to flag and block fraudulent transactions; Example: Banks use AI to detect suspicious credit card activities.
3.  **Algorithmic Trading:** AI-powered systems execute trades at high speeds based on market data and predictive models to optimize investment returns; Example: High-Frequency Trading (HFT) firms.
4.  **Customer Service:** AI-driven chatbots and virtual assistants provide 24/7 support, answering queries and guiding customers through processes; Example: SBI's SIA chatbot.
5.  **Risk Management and Compliance:** AI helps in monitoring and ensuring compliance with regulatory requirements (RegTech) and assessing market risks more effectively.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, AI is fundamentally reshaping the financial landscape, but its adoption must be balanced with robust governance to manage associated risks like bias and security.

### Q5. What are the risks of artificial intelligence in financial intermediation?

**INTRO:** Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming financial intermediation by automating processes, enhancing risk management, and personalizing services for greater efficiency and inclusion.

**BODY:**

1.  **Algorithmic Bias:** AI models trained on historical data can perpetuate and even amplify existing biases, leading to discriminatory outcomes; Example: An AI loan model unfairly denying credit to certain demographic groups.
2.  **Lack of Transparency:** The "black box" nature of complex AI models makes it difficult to understand or explain their decision-making process, posing a challenge for accountability.
3.  **Cybersecurity Threats:** Centralized AI systems can become a prime target for sophisticated cyberattacks, potentially leading to large-scale data breaches or financial disruption.
4.  **Systemic Risk:** The widespread use of similar AI models by multiple financial institutions could lead to herd behavior and amplify market shocks, creating systemic risk.
5.  **Job Displacement:** Automation of routine tasks like data entry, customer service, and credit analysis could lead to significant job losses in the financial sector.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, AI is fundamentally reshaping the financial landscape, but its adoption must be balanced with robust governance to manage associated risks like bias and security.
