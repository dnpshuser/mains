### Q. What are different types of irrigation system in India ?

**INTRO:** Irrigation, the artificial application of water to land, is vital for Indian agriculture, with about 50% of the net sown area currently under irrigation. Ensuring 'Har Khet Ko Pani' (water to every field) is a key government objective to enhance productivity and farmer resilience.

**BODY:**

1.  **Well and Tubewell Irrigation:** Using groundwater for irrigation; Example: Most dominant form, accounting for over 60% of net irrigated area, prevalent in the Indo-Gangetic plains of Punjab and UP.
2.  **Canal Irrigation:** Diverting water from rivers or reservoirs through a network of canals; Example: Indira Gandhi Canal in Rajasthan, which has transformed desert land into arable fields, is the second largest source.
3.  **Tank Irrigation:** Storing rainwater in natural or man-made depressions/tanks; Example: A traditional method, prominent in peninsular states like Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh for rice cultivation.
4.  **Drip Irrigation:** Applying water directly to the root zone through a network of pipes and emitters; Example: A micro-irrigation technique widely used for horticultural crops in states like Maharashtra and Karnataka.
5.  **Sprinkler Irrigation:** Spraying water over the fields, simulating rainfall; Example: Suitable for sandy soils and undulating terrain, used for groundnut and gram in Rajasthan and Haryana.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, modernizing and expanding the irrigation network through sustainable water management practices is imperative for achieving food security and doubling farmers' income.

**BODY:**

1.  **Dams and Reservoirs:** Large-scale storage created by obstructing a river's flow; Example: Bhakra-Nangal Dam on Sutlej river, which provides irrigation and power to several states.
2.  **Tanks and Ponds:** Small, localized surface water bodies, often community-managed; Example: Traditional 'Eris' and 'Ooranis' in Tamil Nadu used for storing monsoon runoff for irrigation.
3.  **Groundwater Aquifers:** Natural underground layers of water-bearing permeable rock; Example: The vast aquifers of the Indo-Gangetic basin are a primary source for tubewell irrigation.
4.  **Check Dams and Percolation Tanks:** Small, man-made barriers built across streams to capture runoff; Example: Used extensively in watershed management projects like 'Jalyukt Shivar' in Maharashtra to recharge groundwater.
5.  **Farm Ponds (Khet Talab):** Individual farm-level structures to harvest rainwater for protective irrigation; Example: Promoted under schemes like the National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA).

### Q. What are the challenges in Indian irrigation system ?

**INTRO:** Irrigation, the artificial application of water to land, is vital for Indian agriculture, with about 50% of the net sown area currently under irrigation. Ensuring 'Har Khet Ko Pani' (water to every field) is a key government objective to enhance productivity and farmer resilience.

**BODY:**

1.  **Irrigation Potential Gap:** A significant gap between Irrigation Potential Created (IPC) and Irrigation Potential Utilized (IPU); Example: Due to poor maintenance of canals and distributaries, the utilization is only about 80% of the created potential.
2.  **Groundwater Over-exploitation:** Unsustainable extraction of groundwater for irrigation leading to falling water tables; Example: Over 60% of districts in the Green Revolution belt of Punjab and Haryana are water-stressed.
3.  **Low Water Use Efficiency:** Predominance of inefficient flood irrigation methods leading to high water wastage; Example: India's water use efficiency is only 38%, compared to 50-60% in developed nations.
4.  **Waterlogging and Salinity:** Excessive irrigation in canal command areas without proper drainage; Example: Large tracts of land in Uttar Pradesh and Haryana have become unproductive due to soil salinization.
5.  **Inter-State Water Disputes:** Conflicts between states over the sharing of river water; Example: The Cauvery water dispute between Karnataka and Tamil Nadu frequently affects the release of water for irrigation.

**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, modernizing and expanding the irrigation network through sustainable water management practices is imperative for achieving food security and doubling farmers' income.

5.  **National Water Mission:** One of the missions under the National Action Plan on Climate Change; Example: Aims to conserve water, minimize wastage, and ensure more equitable distribution through integrated water resources management.

### Q. What are the importance of microirrigation in India ?

**INTRO:** Microirrigation, encompassing drip and sprinkler systems, is a 'More Crop Per Drop' approach that delivers water directly to the plant's root zone. With India facing increasing water stress, microirrigation is a critical tool for sustainable agriculture, currently covering about 13 million hectares.

**BODY:**

1.  **Water Conservation:** Achieves significant water savings (30-70%) compared to conventional flood irrigation; Example: Drip irrigation for sugarcane in Maharashtra has reduced water consumption by up to 60%.
2.  **Increased Crop Productivity:** Leads to higher yields due to efficient water and nutrient delivery; Example: Studies show yield increases of 20-50% for various fruits and vegetables under micro-irrigation.
3.  **Energy Efficiency:** Requires less energy for pumping water due to lower pressure requirements; Example: Farmers can save up to 30% on electricity bills compared to operating high-capacity pumps for flood irrigation.
4.  **Improved Fertilizer Use Efficiency:** Allows for 'fertigation', the precise application of fertilizers with water, reducing wastage; Example: Fertigation can reduce fertilizer consumption by 25-40%, lowering input costs and preventing soil degradation.
5.  **Cultivation on Undulating Land:** Enables irrigation on uneven terrain and sandy soils where flood irrigation is not feasible; Example: Sprinkler systems are highly effective for irrigating groundnut and mustard in the sandy soils of Rajasthan.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, accelerating the adoption of microirrigation through financial support, technological innovation, and farmer awareness is essential for building a water-efficient and climate-resilient agricultural future.

### Q. What are the challenges of microirrigation in India ?

**INTRO:** Microirrigation, encompassing drip and sprinkler systems, is a 'More Crop Per Drop' approach that delivers water directly to the plant's root zone. With India facing increasing water stress, microirrigation is a critical tool for sustainable agriculture, currently covering about 13 million hectares.

**BODY:**

1.  **High Initial Cost:** The capital investment for installing drip or sprinkler systems is high for small and marginal farmers; Example: The cost can range from ₹50,000 to ₹1,00,000 per hectare, which is prohibitive despite subsidies.
2.  **Clogging of Emitters:** Emitters in drip systems are prone to clogging due to silt, algae, or salt precipitation; Example: This requires regular maintenance and filtration systems, adding to the operational complexity and cost.
3.  **Lack of Technical Know-how:** Many farmers lack the knowledge for proper design, operation, and maintenance of the systems; Example: Incorrect installation can lead to non-uniform water distribution, affecting crop growth.
4.  **Unreliable Power Supply:** Intermittent and poor-quality electricity supply in rural areas hampers the operation of the systems; Example: Frequent power cuts disrupt the irrigation schedule, which is critical for fertigation.
5.  **Suitability for a Limited Range of Crops:** Microirrigation is most suitable for wide-spaced, high-value horticultural crops, not for closely spaced crops like wheat and paddy.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, accelerating the adoption of microirrigation through financial support, technological innovation, and farmer awareness is essential for building a water-efficient and climate-resilient agricultural future.

**BODY:**

2.  **Micro Irrigation Fund (MIF):** A dedicated fund of ₹5,000 crore created with NABARD to facilitate state-level promotion; Example: States can access this fund at a concessional rate of interest to expand micro-irrigation coverage.
3.  **Promotion through State Missions:** State governments have launched their own missions to supplement central efforts; Example: The Andhra Pradesh Micro-Irrigation Project (APMIP) is one of the most successful state-led initiatives.
4.  **Awareness and Training Programs:** The government organizes demonstrations, training camps, and workshops for farmers; Example: Krishi Vigyan Kendras (KVKs) play a key role in disseminating knowledge about the benefits and usage of these systems.
5.  **GST Reduction:** The Goods and Services Tax (GST) on micro-irrigation equipment has been reduced to encourage adoption; Example: The GST rate was lowered from 18% to 12% to make the systems more affordable.

**CONCLUSION:** Thus, accelerating the adoption of microirrigation through financial support, technological innovation, and farmer awareness is essential for building a water-efficient and climate-resilient agricultural future.
