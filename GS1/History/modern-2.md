### Q27. What were different types of land revenue system applied by the British in India ? Explain with their location and features.
- *Intro:*
  - To ensure a stable income, the British introduced three distinct revenue systems that radically altered land ownership in India.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Permanent Settlement (1793)}}}$, **Location**: Primarily in Bengal, Bihar, and Odisha — *Example: Introduced by Lord Cornwallis.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Zamindar as Owner}}}$: The system recognized the landlord as the absolute owner of the land — *Example: Rights to sell or mortgage the land.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Fixed Revenue}}}$: The state's share was fixed forever, providing no incentive for improvement — *Example: 10/11th of the rent went to the state.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Sunset Law}}}$: Zamindars lost their land if revenue was not paid by sunset on a specific day — *Example: Frequent auctioning of estates in Bengal.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ryotwari System (1820)}}}$, **Location**: Madras, Bombay, and parts of Assam — *Example: Developed by Thomas Munro and Alexander Reed.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Direct Contract}}}$: The state dealt directly with the individual cultivator (Ryot) — *Example: Elimination of the middleman Zamindar.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Temporary Assessment}}}$: Revenue was revised periodically, usually every 20-30 years — *Example: High rates often leading to peasant distress.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ownership Rights}}}$: Ryots were owners as long as they paid the revenue — *Example: Transferability of land titles.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Mahalwari System (1833)}}}$, **Location**: North-Western Provinces, Central India, and Punjab — *Example: Modified by Holt Mackenzie and Lord William Bentinck.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Village as Unit}}}$: Revenue was settled with the entire village community (Mahal) collectively — *Example: The Lambardar (Village Head) collected the revenue.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Joint Responsibility}}}$: Villagers were jointly and severally responsible for the total revenue — *Example: Community-based tax sharing.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Flexible Rates}}}$: Based on the potential of the land, though often set at 50-60% of produce — *Example: Frequent reassessments in the Punjab region.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Regardless of the form, all systems prioritized high extraction, leading to the systemic impoverishment of the Indian peasantry.

### Q31. Explain the drain of wealth policy adopted by British.
- *Intro:*
  - The "Drain of Wealth" refers to the constant, unilateral flow of wealth from India to Britain for which India received no adequate economic return.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Home Charges}}}$: Payments made by the Government of India in London — *Example: Salaries of the Secretary of State and his office.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Military Expenditure}}}$: India paid for British wars fought outside Indian borders — *Example: Costs of the Afghan and Burmese wars.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Interest on Debt}}}$: India was charged interest on the "Public Debt" created to fund its own conquest — *Example: Debt incurred during the 1857 revolt suppression.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Private Remittances}}}$: British officers and traders sent their savings and profits back to England — *Example: Remittance of pensions and salaries by ICS officers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Dividends on Capital}}}$: Guaranteed high interest was paid to British investors in Indian projects — *Example: The 5% guaranteed interest on Railway investments.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Council Bills}}}$: A mechanism to transfer Indian revenue to London via the trade system — *Example: Selling bills in London to be redeemed in Indian rupees.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Loss of Bullion}}}$: India’s trade surplus was used to pay for 'invisible' services, leading to a net loss of gold/silver — *Example: Unilateral transfer of treasure.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Banking and Insurance}}}$: British firms controlled all commercial services, taking away high profits — *Example: Dominance of 'Exchange Banks'.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Stores Purchase}}}$: The Indian government purchased all its supplies (from rails to paper) from Britain — *Example: Exclusion of Indian manufacturers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Capital Consumption}}}$: The drain prevented the formation of local capital for Indian industrialization — *Example: Lack of domestic investment funds.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - First exposed by Dadabhai Naoroji, the drain theory provided a scientific basis for Indian nationalism by linking poverty to foreign rule.

  - The systemic drain of wealth resulted in the chronic underdevelopment of the Indian economy and the impoverishment of its people.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Capital Scarcity}}}$: India lacked the capital required to build modern industries and infrastructure — *Example: Dependence on foreign loans for railways.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Chronic Poverty}}}$: The majority of the population lived at a subsistence level with no reserves — *Example: Low per-capita income estimates by Digby and Naoroji.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Agrarian Stagnation}}}$: Lack of capital for investment in better seeds, fertilizers, or irrigation — *Example: Continued use of medieval farming techniques.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rising Debt}}}$: The "Public Debt of India" ballooned as Britain charged India for the cost of its own empire — *Example: Debt reaching £224 million by 1900.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Taxation Burden}}}$: Extremely high taxes on essential items to meet 'Home Charges' — *Example: The Salt Tax affecting the poorest.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Industrial Backwardness}}}$: Indian entrepreneurs could not compete with British capital supported by the state — *Example: Early failure of many Indian shipping and steel ventures.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Unemployment}}}$: Decline in local industries without the rise of new ones led to massive joblessness — *Example: Ruin of the weaver and artisan classes.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Famine Vulnerability}}}$: The drain removed the economic "buffer" that could have saved lives during droughts — *Example: 19th-century famines killing nearly 15 million people.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Brain Drain}}}$: High-paying administrative and technical jobs were reserved for Europeans — *Example: Exclusion of Indians from the ICS and Engineering services.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Political Subservience}}}$: Economic dependence ensured that India remained politically tied to British interests — *Example: Manipulation of currency and trade policy.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - The drain was the "bleeding of the Indian heart," ensuring that India remained a rural slum for the benefit of a wealthy London.

### Q34. What were the socio cultural policy adopted by the British ? 
- *Intro:*
  - British socio-cultural policy shifted from non-interference (Orientalism) to an aggressive civilizing mission (Utilitarianism and Evangelicalism).
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Western Education}}}$: Promoting English as the medium of instruction to create "brown sahibs" — *Example: Macaulay’s Minute of 1835.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Social Legislation}}}$: Laws enacted to abolish regressive Indian customs — *Example: The Abolition of Sati (1829) and Widow Remarriage Act (1856).*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Orientalist Support}}}$: Early policy of studying and preserving Indian classical languages and laws — *Example: Founding of the Asiatic Society by William Jones.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Missionary Activity}}}$: Allowing Christian missionaries to operate and convert in India — *Example: Charter Act of 1813 removing restrictions on missionaries.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Downward Filtration}}}$: Educating a small elite group with the hope that knowledge would "filter down" to the masses — *Example: Focus on universities over primary schools.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Legal Uniformity}}}$: Codifying Indian laws into a single Western framework — *Example: The work of the Law Commission under Lord Macaulay.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Press Regulation}}}$: Alternating between liberalizing and gagging the Indian press — *Example: Metcalfe's liberalization vs. Lytton’s Press Act.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Archaeological Preservation}}}$: Systematic mapping and restoration of Indian historical sites — *Example: Founding of the ASI under Lord Curzon.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Infanticide Prohibition}}}$: Legislation to ban female infanticide in various regions — *Example: Regulation of 1795 and 1804.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Thuggee Suppression}}}$: Campaign to eliminate the cult of ritualistic highway robbers — *Example: Operations led by William Sleeman.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - These policies were designed to modernize India just enough to serve administrative needs while asserting European cultural superiority.

### Q35. What were the impacts of socio cultural policy of the British in India ?
- *Intro:*
  - British policies had a contradictory impact, sparking both a modern intellectual awakening and a reactionary religious revival.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Intellectual Awakening}}}$: Exposure to Western ideas of liberty and democracy led to the Indian Renaissance — *Example: The rise of leaders like Raja Ram Mohan Roy.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rise of Middle Class}}}$: Emergence of a professional class (lawyers, doctors, clerks) who became the backbone of nationalism — *Example: Founders of the Indian National Congress.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Social Reform}}}$: Successful legal bans on inhumane practices changed the social fabric — *Example: Gradual decline of Sati and child marriage.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Modern Education}}}$: Established a network of schools and colleges based on the Western model — *Example: Founding of Bombay, Madras, and Calcutta Universities (1857).*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Alienation from Culture}}}$: Created a segment of society that was "Indian in blood but English in taste" — *Example: The "Anglicized" urban elite.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious Reaction}}}$: Perceived attacks on religion led to conservative backlashes and revivalist movements — *Example: The 1857 revolt and the later Arya Samaj.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Communal Identity}}}$: British census and separate electorates hardened religious divisions — *Example: Rise of the "Divide and Rule" politics.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Vernacular Growth}}}$: Paradoxically, the study of local languages by Orientalists helped in the growth of modern Indian literature — *Example: Development of modern Bengali and Marathi prose.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Legal Equality (Theoretical)}}}$: Introduced the concept of "Rule of Law" as opposed to arbitrary feudal rule — *Example: Uniform criminal code for the whole country.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{National Consciousness}}}$: English provided a common language for leaders from different regions to communicate — *Example: Leaders from Madras and Punjab meeting in Congress.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - British socio-cultural policy acted as the "unconscious tool of history," dismantling old structures and providing the tools for India's modern national identity.

### Q36. What were the impacts of Industrial revolution in India ?
- *Intro:*
  - The Industrial Revolution in Britain acted as a destructive force for India, turning it from a producer of manufactured goods into a consumer of British products.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Deindustrialization}}}$: Cheap, machine-made British textiles flooded the Indian market, destroying local weavers — *Example: The decline of traditional weaving centers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Export of Raw Materials}}}$: India became a supplier of cheap raw materials for British factories — *Example: Export of raw cotton, jute, and indigo.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Railway Expansion}}}$: Built to facilitate the deep penetration of British goods and extraction of resources — *Example: Linking the interior cotton-growing regions to ports.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Commercialization of Agriculture}}}$: Farmers were forced to grow crops needed by British industry — *Example: Shift from rice/wheat to Indigo for the dye industry.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Destruction of Self-Sufficiency}}}$: The village economy's balance between agriculture and craft was permanently broken — *Example: Artisans becoming landless laborers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rise of Plantation Economy}}}$: European capital flowed into tea, coffee, and rubber estates — *Example: Exploitative labor conditions in Assam tea gardens.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{One-way Trade}}}$: Abolition of protective duties on British imports while Indian exports faced high tariffs in UK — *Example: The Charter Act of 1833.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Economic Drainage}}}$: The surplus generated in India was siphoned off to fund further industrialization in Britain — *Example: Home Charges.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Growth of Port Cities}}}$: Wealth shifted from interior craft centers to coastal export hubs — *Example: Rise of Bombay and Calcutta at the expense of Murshidabad.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Capital Flight}}}$: Profits from the Indian market were not reinvested in India but repatriated to London — *Example: Repatriated profits of British banks.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - While the Industrial Revolution modernized Britain, it "de-developed" India, ensuring it remained an agrarian colony for over a century.

### Q37. Describe the role of eminent governor generals towards building East India empire.
- *Intro:*
  - The expansion of the British Empire in India was driven by the specific strategic and administrative policies of several key Governor-Generals.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Warren Hastings (1773-85)}}}$: Laid the administrative foundation and successfully defended the EIC against regional powers — *Example: Dealing with the Marathas and Mysore during the First Anglo-Maratha war.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Cornwallis (1786-93)}}}$: Established the "Steel Frame" of the bureaucracy and fixed the revenue system — *Example: Introduction of the Permanent Settlement and Civil Service reforms.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Wellesley (1798-1805)}}}$: Transformed the EIC into the paramount power through aggressive diplomacy — *Example: The system of Subsidiary Alliance.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Hastings (1813-23)}}}$: Finally crushed the Maratha power and established complete British hegemony over Central India — *Example: The Third Anglo-Maratha War.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord William Bentinck (1828-35)}}}$: Focused on social consolidation and administrative efficiency — *Example: Abolition of Sati and introduction of English education.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Dalhousie (1848-56)}}}$: Completed the map of British India through annexation and modern infrastructure — *Example: Doctrine of Lapse and the first Railway line.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Hardinge}}}$: Fought the First Sikh War, bringing the powerful Punjab state under British influence — *Example: The Treaty of Lahore.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Auckland}}}$: Attempted to secure the North-West frontier, though often through disastrous wars — *Example: The First Anglo-Afghan War.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lord Canning}}}$: Successfully navigated the 1857 revolt and oversaw the transition from Company to Crown rule — *Example: The Government of India Act 1858.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Robert Clive}}}$: (Governor of Bengal) Won the initial foothold for the Company — *Example: The Battle of Plassey and the Diwani rights.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - These men used a combination of war, law, and diplomacy to systematically turn a trading company into the sovereign of the subcontinent.

### Q38. What are the contributions of Ishwarchand Vidyasagar in Indian society ?
- *Intro:*
  - Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar was a towering figure of the 19th-century Bengal Renaissance, known for his relentless fight for social justice and educational reform.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Widow Remarriage}}}$: His greatest achievement was legally validating the marriage of Hindu widows — *Example: His efforts led to the Widow Remarriage Act of 1856.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Women’s Education}}}$: Established dozens of schools for girls at a time when female education was taboo — *Example: Helping found the Bethune School in Calcutta.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Sanskrit Reform}}}$: Opened the doors of the Sanskrit College to non-Brahmin students — *Example: Breaking the priestly monopoly on ancient knowledge.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Bengali Prose}}}$: Modernized and standardized the Bengali alphabet and prose — *Example: His book 'Barnaparichay' is still used to teach the language.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Opposition to Polygamy}}}$: Campaigned vigorously against the practice of 'Kulin' polygamy in Bengal — *Example: Writing influential pamphlets against multiple marriages.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rationalism}}}$: Introduced Western philosophy and logic alongside traditional Sanskrit studies — *Example: Modernizing the curriculum of the Sanskrit College.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Child Marriage}}}$: Was a vocal opponent of child marriage and worked to raise the age of consent — *Example: Organizing public petitions and legal arguments.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Humanitarianism}}}$: Spent a large portion of his salary on charities and disaster relief — *Example: His selfless service during the 1866 Orissa Famine.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Social Equality}}}$: Believed that knowledge was for all, regardless of caste — *Example: Encouraging Dalit students to study the Vedas.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Moral Living}}}$: Set a personal example of simplicity and integrity that inspired the youth — *Example: He was popularly known as 'Daya-r Sagar' (Ocean of Kindness).*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Vidyasagar’s legacy is defined by his synthesis of traditional scholarship and modern humanism, fundamentally altering the status of women in India.

### Q39. Describe the role of Swami Vivekanand in Indian reinessance. 
- *Intro:*
  - Swami Vivekananda was a key figure who introduced Indian spiritualism to the West and brought a sense of self-respect and dynamic action to the Indian Renaissance.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Spiritual Pride}}}$: Restored the confidence of Indians in their own culture after decades of colonial humiliation — *Example: His assertion that Hinduism was a "Universal Religion".*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Parliament of Religions}}}$: Gained international recognition for Indian thought in 1893 — *Example: His famous "Sisters and Brothers of America" speech in Chicago.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Neo-Vedanta}}}$: Reinterpreted the Upanishads to emphasize practical life and social service over mere ritual — *Example: His philosophy of "Practical Vedanta".*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ramakrishna Mission}}}$: Founded an organization to combine spiritual life with social service (Daridra-Narayan) — *Example: Service to the poor during plagues and famines.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Youth Inspiration}}}$: Called upon the youth to be "strong of body and mind" for national service — *Example: His quote, "Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached".*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Secularism}}}$: Promoted the idea of harmony between all religions — *Example: His belief that all paths lead to the same truth.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Scientific Synthesis}}}$: Argued that Indian spirituality and Western science were complementary — *Example: His dialogues with scientists like Nikola Tesla.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Caste Critique}}}$: Severely criticized the "don't-touchism" of orthodox caste practices — *Example: Advocating for the upliftment of the masses.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Nationalism}}}$: Though not directly in politics, his ideas of self-reliance influenced every major freedom fighter — *Example: Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose calling him the "Maker of Modern India".*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Education}}}$: Advocated for a "man-making" and "character-building" education — *Example: Focus on self-reliance and moral courage.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Vivekananda transformed a passive society into a dynamic one, providing the moral and spiritual fuel for the subsequent political struggle for independence.

### Q40. Give an account on socio religious reform movements post 18th Century in India.

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Arya Samaj (1875)}}}$: A revivalist movement led by Dayanand Saraswati that emphasized the Vedas — *Example: Motto of "Go back to the Vedas" and Shuddhi movement.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Prarthana Samaj (1867)}}}$: A reform movement in Maharashtra focused on social equality — *Example: Led by Atmaram Pandurang and M.G. Ranade.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Aligarh Movement}}}$: Aimed at modernizing the Muslim community through Western education — *Example: Founded by Sir Syed Ahmad Khan.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Satyashodhak Samaj (1873)}}}$: Fought against caste oppression and for the rights of Bahujans — *Example: Founded by Jyotirao Phule.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Theosophical Society}}}$: Promoted the study of ancient religions and universal brotherhood — *Example: Led by Annie Besant in India.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Self-Respect Movement}}}$: A radical anti-caste movement in South India — *Example: Founded by E.V. Ramaswamy (Periyar).*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Deoband Movement}}}$: An orthodox Islamic revivalist movement focused on religious education — *Example: Establishment of the Darul Uloom in 1866.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Singh Sabha Movement}}}$: Worked for the reform of Sikh gurdwaras and modernization of the community — *Example: Founding of Khalsa colleges.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Servants of India Society}}}$: Dedicated to social service and the training of national missionaries — *Example: Founded by Gopal Krishna Gokhale.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Young Bengal Movement}}}$: Radical intellectual group that challenged all traditions — *Example: Led by Henry Vivian Derozio.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rahnumai Mazdayasnan Sabha}}}$: Aimed at restoring the purity of the Zoroastrian religion — *Example: Led by Naoroji Furdonji and Dadabhai Naoroji.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - These movements collectively laid the intellectual foundation for modern India, ensuring that political freedom would be accompanied by social progress.
