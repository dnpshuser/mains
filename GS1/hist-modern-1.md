### Q1. Even after a good early start by Portuguese, they were not able to compete with their counterparts. Give reasons.
- *Intro:*
  - Despite early maritime success, the Portuguese failed to sustain power in India because they lacked resources, local alliances and strategic focus.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Limited Resources}}}$: Portugal had a small population and could not match the military and financial strength of Britain and the Dutch — *Example: Inability to provide reinforcements during the 17th-century conflicts.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Coastal focus}}}$: Portuguese control remained limited to ports and forts, without deeper penetration into the Indian hinterland — *Example: Their presence was largely restricted to Goa, Daman, and Diu.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Naval setbacks}}}$: Superior naval technology and strategy of the British and Dutch weakened their Indian Ocean position — *Example: The defeat at the Battle of Swally in 1612.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Dutch Hegemony}}}$: The rise of the Dutch East India Company directly challenged and seized Portuguese territories — *Example: The Dutch capture of Cochin in 1663.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Poor diplomacy}}}$: They failed to build lasting alliances with major Indian powers like the Mughal court or Marathas — *Example: Frequent clashes with the Marathas over coastal territories.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious intolerance}}}$: Forced conversions and the Goa Inquisition created deep local hostility and resistance — *Example: Persecution of Hindus and Muslims in Goa.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Global overreach}}}$: The discovery of Brazil diverted Portuguese attention and investment away from the Indian colonies — *Example: Shift of imperial focus to South American gold and sugar.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Corruption}}}$: High-level officials engaged in private trade rather than serving the crown, leading to a bankrupt administration — *Example: Embezzlement by Viceroys and governors.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Spanish Annexation}}}$: The union of the Spanish and Portuguese crowns (1580–1640) dragged Portugal into Spain’s European wars — *Example: Being targeted by English and Dutch forces as Spanish enemies.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Trade Monopoly Failure}}}$: Their inability to maintain a monopoly on spices as competitors established direct trade links — *Example: The loss of the spice trade to the Dutch VOC.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Small Scale Operations}}}$: Unlike the British EIC, the Portuguese 'Estado da India' was state-controlled and rigid — *Example: Lack of private merchant initiative compared to English joint-stock companies.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Discovery of the New World}}}$: Focus on Atlantic trade routes made the Indian Ocean route less lucrative for the Portuguese crown over time — *Example: Increased competition in the Atlantic sugar trade.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - The Portuguese could not compete because their empire was too small, too religiously rigid and too isolated against stronger European rivals.

### Q2. What are the impacts of Portuguese in India.
- *Intro:*
  - Portuguese rule left a lasting imprint on Indian politics, economy, society and culture through trade, religion and administration.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Viceroy System}}}$: Introduced a centralized colonial administrative model via a crown-appointed governor — *Example: Francisco de Almeida as the first Viceroy.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Agricultural Innovation}}}$: Introduced numerous New World crops that transformed Indian agriculture and diet — *Example: Introduction of tobacco, potato, tomato, and cashew.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Printing Press}}}$: Established the first printing press in India, facilitating the spread of knowledge — *Example: The first book printed in Goa in 1556.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Direct Trade Link}}}$: Bypassed the Middle Eastern land routes to establish direct maritime trade with Europe — *Example: The Cape Route spice trade to Lisbon.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious Spread}}}$: Established Christianity as a major religion in coastal regions — *Example: The missionary work of St. Francis Xavier.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Social Reform}}}$: Attempted early social reforms like the condemnation of Sati in their territories — *Example: Albuquerque’s ban on Sati in Goa in 1510.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Architecture Style}}}$: Introduced the "Baroque" and Iberian architectural elements — *Example: The Basilica of Bom Jesus in Goa.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Naval Superiority}}}$: Introduced advanced naval warfare techniques and shipborne artillery — *Example: The use of multi-decked ships with cannons.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Cartaz System}}}$: Introduced the concept of maritime licensing and state-controlled sea lanes — *Example: Mandatory passes for any merchant ship in the Indian Ocean.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Linguistic Impact}}}$: Influenced local languages, particularly Konkani and Malayalam — *Example: Portuguese loanwords like 'Almari' (cupboard) and 'Chabi' (key).*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Missionary Education}}}$: Founded some of the earliest Western-style schools and colleges — *Example: St. Paul’s College in Goa.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Blue Water Policy}}}$: Demonstrated that control of the high seas was key to imperial success in India — *Example: Domination of the Arabian Sea trade routes.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Scientific Studies}}}$: Promoted the study of Indian medicinal plants and botany — *Example: Garcia de Orta’s work on Indian "Simples and Drugs".*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Portuguese influence shaped Indian trade, religion and coastal culture, while introducing European institutions, new crops and early colonial administration.

### Q3. Significance of Vasco’s entry in India ?
- *Intro:*
  - Vasco da Gama’s arrival in India opened the sea route from Europe, marking the start of direct European-Indian trade and colonisation.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Maritime Route}}}$: Established the "Cape Route," bypassing the Ottoman-controlled land routes — *Example: Connecting Lisbon to Calicut via the Cape of Good Hope.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Trade Revolution}}}$: Initiated direct spice trade, significantly reducing costs and increasing European access — *Example: Bringing pepper and cinnamon directly to the Atlantic coast.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{End of Monopoly}}}$: Successfully challenged the Arab and Venetian monopoly over the eastern trade — *Example: Undercutting the prices of spice merchants in the Mediterranean.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Colonial Era Beginning}}}$: Marked the start of the "European Era" in Indian history and territorial conquest — *Example: Setting the stage for the establishment of the 'Estado da India'.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Naval Command}}}$: Opened the Indian Ocean to European naval power and "gunboat diplomacy" — *Example: Subsequent Portuguese bombardment of Calicut.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Diplomatic Relations}}}$: Initiated the first formal diplomatic contacts between a European kingdom and Indian states — *Example: Vasco's meeting with the Zamorin of Calicut.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Cultural Exchange}}}$: Introduced Western commodities and Christian missions to the Indian subcontinent — *Example: Arrival of Jesuit missions following the trade routes.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Global Center Shift}}}$: Contributed to the shift of the economic center from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic — *Example: The rise of Lisbon as a global trade hub.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Geographical Knowledge}}}$: Provided Europe with accurate navigational maps and data of the Indian coast — *Example: Documentation of monsoon wind patterns for sea voyages.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Armed Trade}}}$: Set the precedent for "armed commerce" where merchants were also soldiers — *Example: Portuguese ships being equipped with cannons for trade security.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Introduction of Bullion}}}$: Started the massive inflow of European gold and silver into India in exchange for goods — *Example: High demand for Indian textiles paid for in bullion.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Vasco’s entry transformed India into a key theatre of European trade and imperial rivalry.

### Q4. Why the French lost in India ?
- *Intro:*
  - The French failed in India because their global wars, weak naval support and poor local alliances undermined their colonial ambitions.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Government Control}}}$: The French company was a state department, leading to bureaucratic delays and lack of private initiative — *Example: Every decision required approval from the French crown in Paris.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Financial Weakness}}}$: While the British EIC was a wealthy private corporation, the French company often depended on government subsidies — *Example: Bankruptcy of the French company during the Seven Years' War.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Naval Inferiority}}}$: The British Royal Navy maintained superior communication and supply lines from Europe — *Example: The French fleet's inability to provide cover during the siege of Pondicherry.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Strategic Focus}}}$: Dupleix sought the "key to India" in the South, while Clive found it in the wealthy province of Bengal — *Example: British capture of Bengal in 1757 provided infinite resources for war.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Recall of Dupleix}}}$: The French government recalled their most brilliant administrator at a critical juncture — *Example: Dupleix's recall in 1754 demoralized the French forces.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Settlement Geography}}}$: British bases (Calcutta, Madras, Bombay) were more strategically significant than French ones (Pondicherry, Chandernagore) — *Example: Lack of a major deep-water port comparable to Bombay.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Lack of Reinforcements}}}$: During the Seven Years' War, the French could not send supplies through British-controlled oceans — *Example: Isolation of French garrisons due to the British naval blockade.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Internal Dissent}}}$: Constant friction between the French Governor and the naval commanders — *Example: The public rivalry between Dupleix and La Bourdonnais.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Decisive Military Defeat}}}$: The loss at Wandiwash sealed the fate of the French in India — *Example: Sir Eyre Coote defeating the French under Lally in 1760.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{European Preoccupations}}}$: France was more concerned with its continental European borders than its overseas colonies — *Example: Vast resources spent on European wars like the War of Austrian Succession.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Mughal Relations}}}$: The British were more successful in securing favorable farmans and legal standing from the Mughals — *Example: Farrukhsiyar’s Farman of 1717 granted to the British.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Commercial Neglect}}}$: The French focused too much on politics and too little on trade, leading to a lack of local capital — *Example: Decline in French commercial volume compared to the EIC.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Leadership Vacuum}}}$: After Dupleix and Bussy, the French lacked commanders of the caliber of Robert Clive or Eyre Coote — *Example: The inconsistent leadership of Count de Lally.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Strategic overreach, naval weakness and superior British organisation caused the French to lose in India.

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Subsidiary Alliance Model}}}$: Dupleix was the first to use European troops in local Indian dynastic disputes — *Example: Providing French support to Muzaffar Jang in the Deccan.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Catholic Missions}}}$: Spread of Christianity through French missionary orders — *Example: The Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception in Pondicherry.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Medical Institutions}}}$: Established high-quality medical training centers — *Example: The École de Médicine de Pondichéry, now part of JIPMER.*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Layout}}}$: Introduced the "Commune" system of local administration — *Example: Organization of Puducherry into several communes.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - French rule was small-scale but left a lasting imprint in architecture, language, trade networks and colonial culture in South India.

### Q6. What were the causes of decline of Mughals post Aurangzeb ?
- *Intro:*
  - After Aurangzeb, the Mughal Empire weakened rapidly due to internal decay, regional revolts and external invasions.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious Policies}}}$: The reimposition of Jizya and destruction of temples alienated the Rajputs and other allies — *Example: The revolt of the Rathore and Sisodia clans.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Deccan Ulcer}}}$: Aurangzeb’s 26-year campaign in the South drained the state’s finances and military energy — *Example: Bankruptcy of the royal exchequer by 1707.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Weak Successors}}}$: Later Mughal emperors were mere puppets in the hands of powerful nobles — *Example: The "Sayyid Brothers" acting as kingmakers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Jagirdari Crisis}}}$: A shortage of good lands to assign to nobles led to corruption and intense rivalry — *Example: Competition for fertile jagirs in the North.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rise of Regional Powers}}}$: Autonomous "Successor States" stopped remitting revenue to Delhi — *Example: Formation of independent Hyderabad and Bengal.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Foreign Invasions}}}$: Devastating raids broke the empire’s prestige and economy — *Example: Nadir Shah’s invasion in 1739 and the looting of the Peacock Throne.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Maratha Expansion}}}$: Constant pressure from the Marathas across Central India eroded Mughal territory — *Example: Baji Rao I reaching the outskirts of Delhi in 1737.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Decay}}}$: Corruption in the Mansabdari system led to a decrease in military strength — *Example: Officers maintaining fewer troops than their rank required.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Peasant Unrest}}}$: High taxation led to widespread rebellions by agrarian communities — *Example: Revolts by the Jats, Satnamis, and Sikhs.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Wars of Succession}}}$: Frequent and violent civil wars among royal claimants depleted the treasury — *Example: The fratricidal wars following Aurangzeb's death.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Military Stagnation}}}$: Failure to modernize weaponry or tactics compared to the European-trained armies — *Example: Continued reliance on heavy cavalry.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Inertia of the Nobility}}}$: The elite became luxury-loving and lost their martial vigor — *Example: The "rangeela" lifestyle of Muhammad Shah.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Fiscal Strain}}}$: The collapse of the central revenue system as provincial governors retained taxes — *Example: Decline in the 'Khalisa' (crown land) revenue.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - The combination of religious intolerance, fiscal strain and powerful regional challengers caused the Mughal decline after Aurangzeb.


### Q7. How the rise of princely states took place?
- *Intro:*
  - The rise of princely states followed Mughal decline and the emergence of regional powers in the 18th century.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Mughal Fragmentation}}}$: The collapse of central authority created a power vacuum for regional elites — *Example: Decline of Mughal control over distant subas.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Successor States}}}$: Former Mughal governors (Subahdars) declared virtual independence while maintaining nominal loyalty — *Example: Hyderabad (Nizam), Awadh, and Bengal.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Warrior States}}}$: Emerged through armed rebellion against Mughal centralism — *Example: The Maratha Confederacy and the Sikh Misls.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Autonomous Kingdoms}}}$: Ancient regional dynasties that asserted full control during the power vacuum — *Example: Mysore, Travancore, and the Rajput states.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Military Modernization}}}$: Strong local rulers hired European mercenaries to train their armies — *Example: Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan in Mysore.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Fiscal Autonomy}}}$: Local rulers stopped sending 'Peshkash' (tribute) to Delhi and kept revenue for local state-building — *Example: Murshid Quli Khan’s revenue reforms in Bengal.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{European Interference}}}$: British and French support helped some princes stabilize or expand their domains — *Example: EIC's protection of the Nawab of Carnatic.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Regional Identity}}}$: Shift from an imperial culture to diverse regional cultures (Marathi, Odia, Bengali) — *Example: Patronage of local languages in the court of Tanjore.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Breakdown of Mansabdari}}}$: Local elites became hereditary rulers of the lands they previously managed as officers — *Example: The Rise of the Poligars in South India.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Subsidiary Alliance}}}$: Many states rose and were subsequently stabilized through British protection — *Example: The Nizam accepting British protection in 1798.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious Solidarity}}}$: Some states were built around specific religious or ethnic identities — *Example: The Jat kingdom under Suraj Mal.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Weak Mughal authority and active regional leaders led to the formation of princely states that later became the backbone of colonial indirect rule.

### Q9. Role of Robert Clive in winning over India ?

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Political Conspiracy}}}$: Successfully turned the Nawab’s own generals against him — *Example: Dealing with Mir Jafar and Jagat Seth.*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Checking French Influence}}}$: Decisively neutralized French ambitions in the Carnatic region — *Example: Victory at the Battle of Wandiwash.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Corruption Control}}}$: Attempted to curb private trade among Company servants to stabilize revenue — *Example: Forming the 'Society of Trade' in 1765.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Financial Foundations}}}$: Used Bengal's revenue to fund the Company's trade, ending the need for English bullion — *Example: Investing land revenue surplus in export goods.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Strategic Vision}}}$: Identified Bengal as the "Paradise of the Earth" for its strategic and economic depth — *Example: Focusing on the Ganga-Brahmaputra delta.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Clive transformed the EIC from a trading company into a territorial power, paving the way for British dominance in India.

### Q11. Contribution of Dadabhai Naroji.

- *Body:*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{First Indian MP}}}$: Represented Indian interests directly in the British House of Commons — *Example: Elected from Central Finsbury in 1892.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Founder of INC}}}$: Played a key role in establishing the Indian National Congress as a political platform — *Example: Serving as Congress President three times (1886, 1893, 1906).*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Economic Statistics}}}$: Provided the first statistical analysis of India's low per-capita income — *Example: Calculating Indian per-capita income at ₹20 per year in 1867-68.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Demand for Swaraj}}}$: First leader to use the term "Swaraj" from the Congress platform — *Example: The 1906 Calcutta session presidential address.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{East India Association}}}$: Founded organizations in London to lobby for Indian rights among the British public — *Example: Setting up the East India Association in 1866.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Welby Commission}}}$: Served as a member of the royal commission to challenge unfair Indian expenditure — *Example: Advocating for a reduction in military spending.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Social Reformer}}}$: Promoted girls' education and social upliftment among the Parsi community — *Example: Founding the 'Rast Goftar' newspaper.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Indian Civil Service}}}$: Campaigned for simultaneous examinations in India to increase Indian representation — *Example: Fighting against the lowering of the age limit for ICS.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Universal Brotherhood}}}$: Linked Indian struggles with global anti-imperial movements — *Example: Attending the International Socialist Congress in 1904.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Respected by all parties}}}$: 
    - Tilak famously praised him as a *"radical among radicals"*, and acknowledged his pivotal role in openly endorsing the concept of "Swaraj" at the 1906 Calcutta Congress.
    - Bal Gangadhar Tilak, respected Dadabhai Naoroji as a political mentor despite their ideological differences
- *Conclusion:*
  - Naroji made Indian economic suffering visible internationally, boosting the demand for self-rule.

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Neutralizing the French}}}$: Removed French officers and influence from Indian princely courts — *Example: Disbanding the French-trained corps in Hyderabad.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Weakening Marathas}}}$: Exploited internal Maratha disputes to impose British terms — *Example: The Treaty of Bassein in 1802.*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Financial Integration}}}$: Forced Indian states to pay for the British army, reducing the burden on Company finances — *Example: Seizing half of Oudh’s territory for "maintenance" in 1801.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ring Fence Expansion}}}$: Created a buffer zone of dependent states around British provinces — *Example: Protecting Bengal through a dependent Oudh.*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Imperial Image}}}$: Elevated the Governor-General’s status to that of an "Imperial Ruler" — *Example: Building the grand Government House in Calcutta.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Wellesley’s policies turned the EIC into the dominant military and political power in India.

- *Conclusion:*
  - The Sikh struggle ended with annexation but demonstrated the resilience of Punjab against imperial expansion.

### Q16. What were smart policies that British adopted for annexation besides war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The British used diplomacy, legal tools and financial pressure to annex Indian states without open warfare.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Doctrine of Lapse}}}$: Annexing states where the ruler died without a male heir — *Example: Annexation of Satara (1848) and Jhansi (1853) by Dalhousie.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Subsidiary Alliance}}}$: Disarming local states and making them pay for a British force — *Example: Making Hyderabad a dependent state in 1798.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Charge of Misgovernance}}}$: Annexing territories by claiming the local ruler was incompetent — *Example: The annexation of Oudh (Awadh) in 1856.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ring Fence Policy}}}$: Creating a buffer zone of dependent states around British provinces — *Example: Using Oudh to protect Bengal from Marathas.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Pensioning Off Rulers}}}$: Abolishing titles and powers of local kings in exchange for a stipend — *Example: Pensioning of the Nawabs of Carnatic and Bengal.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Strategic Debt}}}$: Lending money to states at high interest and taking land as collateral — *Example: Taking Berar from the Nizam of Hyderabad for debt.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Intervention}}}$: Taking over administration when a ruler was a minor — *Example: Managing Punjab through a Council of Regency.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Treaty Re-interpretation}}}$: Using ambiguous clauses in old treaties to claim new territories — *Example: The various treaties with the Peshwas leading up to 1818.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Cantonment System}}}$: Establishing permanent military bases in princely territories to exert pressure — *Example: Secunderabad and Bangalore cantonments.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Resident's Supremacy}}}$: Turning the British Resident into the de facto ruler of the state — *Example: Interference in the appointment of ministers in Mysore.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - These policies allowed Britain to enlarge its domain by exploiting weakness, dependency and legal loopholes.

### Q17. What were the causes of 1857 war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The 1857 war was triggered by military, political and cultural grievances against British Company rule.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Economic Exploitation}}}$: High land revenue demands and destruction of traditional crafts impoverished the masses — *Example: The impact of the Mahalwari and Ryotwari systems.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Doctrine of Lapse}}}$: The political annexation of states like Jhansi and Satara alienated the ruling class — *Example: Rani Laxmibai joining the revolt.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Annexation of Oudh}}}$: Dismissing the Nawab of Oudh in 1856 deeply hurt the sentiments of the sepoys — *Example: Oudh being the 'nursery of the Bengal Army'.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Sepoy Grievances}}}$: Low pay, racial discrimination, and denial of foreign service allowance — *Example: Comparison of Indian sepoys with British soldiers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Greased Cartridges}}}$: The immediate spark involving cartridges greased with cow and pig fat — *Example: The defiance of Mangal Pandey at Barrackpore.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Religious Interference}}}$: Policies like the Widow Remarriage Act (1856) created fear of forced Christianization — *Example: Missionary activity promoted by the Company.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{General Service Enlistment Act}}}$: Forcing sepoys to serve overseas, which led to "loss of caste" — *Example: Deployment during the Anglo-Afghan wars.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Administrative Exclusion}}}$: Complete exclusion of Indians from high-ranking civil and military posts — *Example: No Indian could rise above the rank of Subedar.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Destruction of Feudal Order}}}$: The removal of Talukdars and Zamindars who lost their traditional social status — *Example: Confiscation of estates in Oudh by the Inam Commission.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Discontinuation of Titles}}}$: Refusal to recognize titles and pensions of former rulers — *Example: Denial of Nana Sahib’s pension.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Misbehavior of British Officials}}}$: Arrogance and physical abuse by British officers toward their Indian subordinates — *Example: Frequent use of racial slurs.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Combined military, social and economic grievances made 1857 a broad-based uprising against British rule.

### Q18. What was the impact of 1857 war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The 1857 war ended Company rule and led to major political and military restructuring in India.
- *Body:*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{End of Company Rule}}}$: The Government of India Act 1858 transferred power to the British Crown — *Example: Creation of the office of the Secretary of State for India.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Queen’s Proclamation}}}$: Guaranteed that the British would no longer annex princely states — *Example: Abandonment of the Doctrine of Lapse.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Army Reorganization}}}$: Implementation of the "Balance and Counter-check" policy — *Example: Increasing the ratio of British to Indian soldiers.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Divide and Rule}}}$: Deliberate policy of playing Muslims and Hindus against each other to prevent unity — *Example: Blaming Muslims for the revolt to curb their influence.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Non-Interference}}}$: The Crown adopted a policy of respecting Indian religious and social customs — *Example: Hesitation in enacting further social reforms like those of Bentinck.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Fiscal Debt}}}$: The cost of suppressing the revolt was added to India's debt — *Example: A debt of £40 million added to the Indian exchequer.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Racial Segregation}}}$: Emergence of "White Only" spaces and a deepened sense of European racial superiority — *Example: Creation of Hill Stations and exclusive Civil Lines.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Collaboration with Princes}}}$: Princely states were now viewed as "breakwaters to the storm" and became loyal allies — *Example: Rewarding the Nizam and Scindia for their support.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Imperial Titles}}}$: The Mughal Emperor was formally deposed, ending the last titular link to the past — *Example: The trial and exile of Bahadur Shah Zafar.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Rise of Nationalism}}}$: Served as a lasting inspiration for future freedom fighters — *Example: Savarkar calling it the "First War of Independence".*
- *Conclusion:*
  - The rebellion reshaped colonial rule, ending Company domination and making British governance more direct and controlled.

### Q20. Enumerate Lytton's aggressive policy and Rippon's to settle down people's aggression in India.

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Arms Act 1878}}}$: Made it a criminal offense for Indians to carry arms without a license while exempting Europeans — *Example: Institutionalizing racial discrimination.*

  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{ICS Age Limit}}}$: Lowered the age limit from 21 to 19 to exclude Indians from civil services — *Example: Making it nearly impossible for Indian students to compete.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Repeal of Press Act}}}$: Ripon restored freedom to the vernacular press in 1882 — *Example: Reversing Lytton’s "Gagging Act".*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Local Self-Government}}}$: Introduced elected municipal boards to involve Indians in administration — *Example: The 1882 Resolution on Local Self-Government.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Ilbert Bill Controversy}}}$: Ripon attempted to allow Indian judges to try Europeans — *Example: The fierce "White Mutiny" against the bill.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{First Factory Act 1881}}}$: Improved working conditions for children and laborers — *Example: Restricting child labor in textile mills.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Hunter Education Commission}}}$: Focused on primary and secondary education for the masses — *Example: Shift from elite to mass education in 1882.*
  - $\color{#7be382}{\textsf{\textbf{Land Revenue Reform}}}$: Attempted to limit state share of land revenue to protect peasants — *Example: Pushing for more humane collection during famines.*
- *Conclusion:*
  - Lytton’s aggression provoked political resistance, while Ripon’s reforms aimed to placate Indians and build trust.
