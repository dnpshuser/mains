### Q1. What do you understand by the phenomenon of 'temperature inversion' in meteorology? How does it affect the weather and the inhabitants of the place?

**INTRO:** Temperature inversion is a meteorological phenomenon where a layer of warm air sits atop a layer of cooler air near the ground, reversing the normal atmospheric condition where temperature decreases with altitude (normal lapse rate). This atmospheric anomaly creates a highly stable condition that significantly impacts local weather and human life.

**BODY:**

1.  **Formation Mechanism:** It typically occurs on long, calm, and clear winter nights. The ground radiates heat and cools rapidly, chilling the air layer in contact with it. The air above remains relatively warm, creating the inversion.
2.  **Atmospheric Stability:** The cold, dense air at the bottom and warm, light air at the top create a very stable atmosphere, preventing vertical mixing of air.

*   **Effects on Weather:**
    1.  **Fog and Smog Formation:** The inversion acts as a "lid," trapping dust, smoke, and water vapor near the ground, leading to the formation of dense fog and smog, especially in urban areas.
    2.  **Reduced Visibility:** The trapped pollutants and fog drastically reduce visibility, affecting air and road transport.
    3.  **Frost Formation:** In the lower cold layer, temperatures can drop below freezing, causing heavy frost that is damaging to crops.
    4.  **Suppression of Convection:** The stability prevents convection, leading to calm, cloudless weather conditions within the inversion layer.

*   **Effects on Inhabitants:**
    1.  **Health Hazards:** Trapped pollutants like PM2.5 lead to a sharp deterioration in air quality, causing respiratory illnesses, allergies, and other health problems.
    2.  **Disruption of Transport:** Poor visibility due to fog disrupts flights, trains, and road traffic, causing economic losses.
    3.  **Agricultural Damage:** Frost can destroy sensitive crops like vegetables and fruits, impacting farmers' livelihoods.
    4.  **Valley Inversions:** In mountainous regions, cold air sinks into valleys at night, creating strong inversions that trap pollution from towns located in the valley floor.



**CONCLUSION:** Temperature inversion is a crucial micro-climatic phenomenon, particularly in winter, that has profound implications for air quality, public health, and economic activities, making its study vital for urban planning and pollution control.

### Q2. Bring out the causes for the formation of heat islands in the urban habitat of the world.

**INTRO:** An Urban Heat Island (UHI) is a metropolitan area that is significantly warmer than its surrounding rural areas. This phenomenon is a distinct form of human-induced climate modification, resulting from the alteration of the land surface and the release of anthropogenic heat.

**BODY:**

1.  **Low Albedo of Surfaces:** Urban surfaces like asphalt roads and dark rooftops have a low albedo (reflectivity) and absorb a high amount of solar radiation, which they re-radiate as heat.
2.  **Urban Geometry (Canyon Effect):** The tall buildings in cities create urban canyons that trap heat by reflecting and absorbing sunlight multiple times. They also reduce wind speed, which would otherwise help in cooling.
3.  **Anthropogenic Heat Release:** Waste heat released from vehicles, air conditioners, industrial processes, and other human activities adds a significant amount of heat to the urban environment.
4.  **Reduced Evapotranspiration:** The lack of vegetation and water bodies in urban areas reduces the natural cooling effect of evapotranspiration.
5.  **Air Pollution:** Pollutants like dust and greenhouse gases in the urban atmosphere can form a dome over the city, trapping outgoing longwave radiation and preventing nighttime cooling.
6.  **Impermeable Surfaces:** Paved surfaces prevent rainwater from percolating into the ground, reducing soil moisture and the potential for evaporative cooling.
7.  **High Thermal Capacity of Materials:** Building materials like concrete and brick have a high capacity to store heat during the day and release it slowly at night, keeping the city warm even after sunset.
8.  **Reduced Sky View Factor:** The dense arrangement of buildings reduces the "sky view factor," limiting the amount of heat that can escape to the colder night sky.
9.  **Impact on Energy Consumption:** UHIs increase the demand for air conditioning, leading to higher energy consumption and a vicious cycle of more waste heat release.
10. **Public Health Impacts:** They exacerbate the effects of heatwaves, leading to increased heat stress and mortality, especially among the elderly and vulnerable.



**CONCLUSION:** The formation of Urban Heat Islands is a serious environmental consequence of unplanned urbanization. Mitigating it requires sustainable urban planning that incorporates green spaces, cool materials, and better energy management.

### Q3. What are non-farm primary activities? How are these activities related to physiographic features in India? Discuss with suitable examples.

**INTRO:** Non-farm primary activities refer to economic activities based on the direct extraction or harvesting of natural resources, other than traditional crop cultivation. In India, the distribution of these activities is intrinsically linked to the country's diverse physiographic features.

**BODY:**

1.  **Fishing:**
    *   **Physiography:** India's long coastline (~7500 km) and extensive continental shelf support marine fishing. Its vast network of rivers, lakes, and reservoirs supports inland fishing.
    *   **Example:** Marine fishing is a major activity along the coasts of **Kerala and Gujarat**. Inland fishing thrives in the **Ganga-Brahmaputra river system**.
2.  **Forestry and Lumbering:**
    *   **Physiography:** This activity is concentrated in the forested mountain and plateau regions.
    *   **Example:** Collection of timber (like Sal and Teak), resin, and minor forest produce is significant in the **Himalayan region** and the forested tracts of **Central India (e.g., Chhattisgarh)**.
3.  **Mining and Quarrying:**
    *   **Physiography:** Mining is directly linked to the geology of the region. The mineral-rich **Chota Nagpur Plateau** and the **Deccan Plateau** are hubs of mining.
    *   **Example:** **Coal mining** in Jharkhand and Odisha, and **iron ore mining** in Karnataka and Goa.
4.  **Animal Husbandry and Pastoralism:**
    *   **Physiography:** Pastoralism is prevalent in regions unsuitable for intensive agriculture, such as arid and semi-arid lands, and high-altitude pastures.
    *   **Example:** Sheep and goat rearing is common in the semi-arid regions of **Rajasthan**. Transhumant pastoralism is practiced by communities like the **Gaddis** in the high-altitude pastures of the **Himalayas**.
5.  **Aquaculture:**
    *   **Physiography:** Brackish water aquaculture (shrimp farming) thrives in coastal areas with lagoons and estuaries. Freshwater aquaculture is practiced in inland ponds and tanks.
    *   **Example:** **Shrimp farming** is a major activity in the coastal plains of **Andhra Pradesh**.



**CONCLUSION:** These non-farm primary activities are crucial for diversifying rural livelihoods and reducing over-dependence on agriculture. Their sustainable management, guided by the specific physiographic context, is essential for balanced regional development.

### Q4. Discuss the distribution and density of population in the Ganga River Basin with special reference to land, soil and water resources. 

**INTRO:** The Ganga River Basin, one of the most extensive and fertile river basins in the world, is also one of the most densely populated regions on Earth. This high population density is a direct consequence of its rich endowment of land, soil, and water resources, which have supported intensive agriculture and human settlement for millennia.

**BODY:**

1.  **Land (Flat Topography):** The basin is a vast, flat alluvial plain, which is easy to cultivate and ideal for the development of transport networks and human settlements. This contrasts with the rugged terrain of the Himalayas or the dissected uplands of the peninsula.
2.  **Soil (Fertile Alluvium):** The rivers of the basin deposit fertile alluvial soil (Khadar and Bhangar), which is rich in nutrients and supports high agricultural productivity. This has allowed the region to sustain a large population for centuries.
3.  **Water (Perennial Rivers):** The Ganga and its tributaries are perennial rivers, fed by both Himalayan glaciers and monsoon rains, ensuring a year-round supply of water for drinking, irrigation, and industry.
4.  **High Population Density:** As a result, the region has a very high population density, often exceeding 500-1000 persons per sq. km, especially in states like Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and West Bengal.
5.  **Intensive Subsistence Agriculture:** The rich resource base supports a pattern of intensive subsistence agriculture, with a focus on food crops like rice and wheat, which can support a large population.
6.  **Distribution within the Basin:**
    *   **Upper Ganga Plain (e.g., Western UP):** High density due to fertile soils and extensive canal irrigation supporting the Green Revolution.
    *   **Middle Ganga Plain (e.g., Bihar):** Extremely high density, but also high poverty, due to population pressure on land and frequent floods.
    *   **Lower Ganga Plain (e.g., West Bengal):** High density, especially in the delta region, supported by rice cultivation and fishing.
7.  **Urbanization:** The basin is home to many ancient and modern cities (e.g., Delhi, Kanpur, Patna, Kolkata) that have grown along the river banks, further concentrating the population.
8.  **Pressure on Resources:** The high population density has put immense pressure on land and water resources, leading to problems like fragmentation of landholdings, groundwater depletion, and severe water pollution.



**CONCLUSION:** The Ganga River Basin is a classic example of how favorable geographical factors can lead to a high concentration of population. However, this has also created a fragile human-environment relationship, making sustainable resource management a critical challenge for the region.

### Q5. Why is the South-West Monsoon called 'Purvaiya' (easterly) in the Bhojpur region? How has this directional seasonal wind system influenced the cultural ethos of the region? 

**INTRO:** In the Bhojpur region of the Middle Ganga Plains (Eastern UP and Bihar), the moisture-laden South-West monsoon winds are paradoxically known as 'Purvaiya' or easterly winds. This is due to the deflection of the monsoon current by the Himalayan barrier, and this wind system is deeply embedded in the region's cultural ethos.

**BODY:**

*   **Geographical Reason for 'Purvaiya':**
    1.  **Bay of Bengal Branch:** The South-West monsoon enters India in two branches. The Bay of Bengal branch moves north-westwards up the Ganga plains.
    2.  **Himalayan Barrier:** The Himalayan mountain range acts as a formidable physical barrier, blocking the northward path of these winds.
    3.  **Deflection of Winds:** Forced by the Himalayas, the monsoon current is deflected and starts flowing westwards along the Ganga valley.
    4.  **Easterly Direction:** In the Bhojpur region, these deflected winds arrive from the east, hence they are perceived and named 'Purvaiya' (from 'Purab' or east).

*   **Influence on Cultural Ethos:**
    1.  **Symbol of Hope and Relief:** The arrival of the Purvaiya signals the end of the scorching summer heat and brings life-giving rains, making it a symbol of hope and rejuvenation.
    2.  **Agricultural Rhythms:** The entire agricultural calendar, from the sowing of paddy to other Kharif crops, is timed with the arrival of these winds.
    3.  **Folklore and Music:** The Purvaiya is a recurring motif in Bhojpuri folk songs (like 'Kajari') and literature, often associated with themes of love, longing, and the pain of separation, as men often migrated for work and were expected to return with the monsoon.
    4.  **Festivals:** Many local festivals and rituals are linked to the monsoon season brought by these winds.
    5.  **Social Life:** The rainy season is a time for community bonding, with activities like sowing and transplanting being done collectively.



**CONCLUSION:** The naming of the monsoon as 'Purvaiya' is a beautiful example of how local geographical perception shapes language. This wind system is not just a climatic phenomenon but the very lifeblood of the Bhojpur region, deeply interwoven with its agriculture, culture, and collective memory.

### Q6. Discuss the meaning of colour-coded weather warnings for cyclone-prone areas given by the India Meteorological Department. 

**INTRO:** The India Meteorological Department (IMD) uses a four-stage, color-coded warning system for cyclones. This system is designed to provide clear, simple, and graded information to disaster management agencies and the public, enabling a calibrated and timely response to minimize loss of life and property.

**BODY:**

1.  **Green Alert ("All is Well"):**
    *   **Meaning:** No adverse weather is expected. It indicates a normal day.
    *   **Action:** No action is required from disaster management agencies or the public.
2.  **Yellow Alert ("Watch"):**
    *   **Meaning:** Issued 2-3 days in advance. It indicates the presence of a cyclonic disturbance and that the weather could worsen.
    *   **Action:** Authorities are advised to "be aware" or "be updated." It is a signal to start monitoring the situation and review preparedness plans.
3.  **Orange Alert ("Alert"):**
    *   **Meaning:** Issued when the cyclone is expected to cause significant disruption. It indicates a high likelihood of adverse weather.
    *   **Action:** Authorities are asked to "be prepared." This is the stage for mobilizing resources, identifying evacuation centers, and disseminating warnings to the fishing community and the public.
4.  **Red Alert ("Warning"):**
    *   **Meaning:** The highest level of warning, issued when the cyclone is imminent and is expected to cause severe damage and risk to life.
    *   **Action:** Authorities are expected to "take action." This involves mass evacuation from low-lying coastal areas, suspension of transport, and positioning of response teams like the NDRF.



**CONCLUSION:** This color-coded system is a crucial component of India's vastly improved cyclone early warning and disaster management framework, which has been successful in drastically reducing cyclone-related fatalities in recent years.

### Q7. Discuss the natural resource potential of the Deccan Trap. 

**INTRO:** The Deccan Traps, a large igneous province formed by massive volcanic eruptions at the end of the Cretaceous period, cover a vast part of peninsular India. The weathering of this basaltic lava over millions of years has created a region with a unique and rich potential for natural resources, particularly soils and minerals.

**BODY:**

1.  **Rich Black Soils (Regur):** The weathering of the basaltic rock has formed deep, clayey black soils. These soils are rich in minerals and have high moisture-retention capacity, making them extremely fertile.
2.  **Cotton Cultivation:** This soil is ideal for the cultivation of cotton, making the Deccan Trap region the "cotton bowl" of India.
3.  **Other Crops:** It also supports the cultivation of other important crops like sugarcane, jowar, and oilseeds.
4.  **Bauxite Deposits:** The process of laterization on the basaltic plateau has led to the formation of rich deposits of bauxite (the ore of aluminum), particularly in Maharashtra and Gujarat.
5.  **Building and Construction Material:** The basalt rock itself is an excellent and widely used material for construction, road building, and as railway ballast.
6.  **Semi-Precious Stones:** The vesicles (gas cavities) in the basalt are often filled with secondary minerals like agate, amethyst, and other zeolites, making the region a source of semi-precious stones.
7.  **Groundwater Resources:** The weathered and fractured layers of the trap rock act as important aquifers, storing significant amounts of groundwater.
8.  **Potential for Geothermal Energy:** The deep geological structure of the region may hold potential for harnessing geothermal energy.



**CONCLUSION:** The Deccan Traps are a prime example of how ancient geological events can create a distinct resource base that shapes the agricultural and industrial economy of a region for millions of years.

### Q8. How will the melting of Himalayan glaciers have a far-reaching impact on the water resources of India? 

**INTRO:** The Himalayan glaciers, often referred to as the "Third Pole," are the source of major North Indian rivers and are receding at an alarming rate due to climate change. This melting will have profound and far-reaching impacts on India's water resources, threatening both water security and food security for millions.

**BODY:**

1.  **Source of Perennial Rivers:** The glaciers are the primary source of water for perennial rivers like the Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra, ensuring their flow during the dry season.
2.  **Short-Term Impact (Increased Flooding):** In the short term, accelerated glacial melt will increase river flows, leading to a higher risk of floods, especially Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs).
3.  **Long-Term Impact (Reduced River Flows):** In the long run, as the glaciers shrink, the summer flow in these rivers will drastically reduce, threatening water availability for drinking, irrigation, and industry.
4.  **Impact on Agriculture:** Reduced water availability will severely impact agriculture in the fertile Indo-Gangetic plains, which depend on these rivers for irrigation, threatening India's food security.
5.  **Hydropower Generation:** The viability of hydropower projects in the Himalayas, which depend on a consistent flow of water, will be at risk.
6.  **Groundwater Recharge:** Reduced river flows will lead to lower rates of groundwater recharge in the plains, exacerbating the problem of groundwater depletion.
7.  **Ecological Impact:** The change in river flows will have a devastating impact on the aquatic ecosystems and biodiversity of the rivers.
8.  **Impact on Navigation:** Reduced water levels could affect inland navigation in waterways like the Ganga.
9.  **Increased Inter-State and International Water Conflicts:** As water becomes scarcer, conflicts over water sharing between states and with neighboring countries (like Pakistan, China, and Bangladesh) are likely to intensify.



**CONCLUSION:** The melting of Himalayan glaciers is a ticking water bomb for India. It necessitates urgent action on both climate change mitigation and the development of adaptation strategies for water resource management.

**INTRO:** The Interlinking of Rivers (ILR) project is an ambitious engineering vision that aims to transfer water from water-surplus river basins to water-deficit ones. **BODY:**

*   **Potential Benefits (Viable Solutions):**
    1.  **Drought Mitigation:** It can provide water to drought-prone regions like western and peninsular India, boosting agricultural productivity.
    2.  **Flood Control:** It can divert excess water from flood-prone rivers like the Ganga and Brahmaputra during the monsoon, mitigating flood damage.
    3.  **Enhanced Irrigation:** The project aims to bring millions of hectares of additional land under irrigation, enhancing food security.
    4.  **Improved Navigation:** It can create a network of navigable inland waterways, promoting cheaper and more eco-friendly transport.
    5.  **Hydropower Generation:** The dams and canals built under the project can be used to generate hydropower.

*   **Challenges and Criticisms (Critical Examination):**
    1.  **Massive Economic Cost:** The project requires an astronomical financial investment, which could be used for other, more efficient water management solutions.
    2.  **Huge Ecological and Environmental Impact:** It can cause irreversible damage to river ecosystems, destroy forests, and affect biodiversity.
    3.  **Large-Scale Displacement of People:** The construction of dams and canals will lead to the displacement of millions of people, creating a massive social problem.

**CONCLUSION:** While the idea of interlinking rivers is appealing, its potential benefits are outweighed by its colossal economic costs and irreversible environmental and social impacts. A more prudent approach would be to focus on decentralized water management solutions like watershed management and improving water use efficiency.

### Q11. Mumbai, Delhi, and Kolkata are the three megacities of the country, but air pollution is a much more serious problem in Delhi compared to the other two. Why is this so ?

**INTRO:** While all three megacities suffer from air pollution, Delhi's problem is significantly more severe due to a unique and unfortunate combination of geographical disadvantages, meteorological conditions, and a higher concentration of pollution sources from its vast hinterland.

**BODY:**

1.  **Geographical Disadvantage (Landlocked):** Delhi is a landlocked city, unlike coastal Mumbai and Kolkata. The sea breeze in Mumbai and Kolkata helps to disperse pollutants, a mechanism that is absent in Delhi.
2.  **Meteorological Conditions (Winter Inversion):** During winter, Delhi experiences strong temperature inversions, where a layer of cool air is trapped under a layer of warm air. This acts as a "lid," preventing pollutants from dispersing vertically.
3.  **Calm Winds:** The winds in North India are generally calm during winter, which leads to the stagnation of pollutants over the city for long periods.
4.  **Pollution from Hinterland:** Delhi is surrounded by a vast agricultural hinterland in Punjab and Haryana. Stubble burning in these states during October-November contributes massively to Delhi's winter pollution.
5.  **Dust from Arid Regions:** Dust storms from the arid regions of Rajasthan and beyond contribute significantly to the particulate matter (PM) levels in Delhi.
6.  **High Vehicular Density:** Delhi has a very high density of private vehicles, which are a major source of vehicular emissions.
7.  **Peripheral Industrial and Thermal Power Plants:** The presence of numerous industries and coal-fired power plants in the National Capital Region (NCR) adds to the pollution load.
8.  **Geographical Funnel:** The Himalayas to the north block the escape of pollutants, effectively trapping them in the Indo-Gangetic plain with Delhi at its center.
9.  **Mumbai's Advantage:** Mumbai benefits from strong sea breezes that regularly flush out pollutants.
10. **Kolkata's Advantage:** Kolkata, being closer to the coast, also benefits from more favorable wind patterns and does not have the problem of large-scale stubble burning in its vicinity.

**CONCLUSION:** Delhi's severe air pollution is a complex problem resulting from a perfect storm of local emissions, regional pollution sources, and adverse geographical and meteorological conditions, making it a particularly challenging "gas chamber."

### Q12. How far do you agree that the behaviour of the Indian monsoon has been changing due to humanizing landscapes ? Discuss.

**INTRO:** I largely agree with the statement. While the Indian monsoon is a large-scale climatic phenomenon driven by global factors, there is growing evidence that human-induced changes in the landscape (humanizing landscapes) at a regional and local level are significantly altering its behavior, particularly its intensity and spatial distribution.

**BODY:**

1.  **Urbanization and Urban Heat Islands (UHIs):** The intense heat generated by cities (UHIs) can create localized convection, leading to more intense, short-duration rainfall events over and downwind of urban areas.
2.  **Deforestation:** Large-scale deforestation, especially in the Western Ghats and the Himalayas, reduces evapotranspiration, which can lead to a decrease in overall rainfall in the region. It also increases surface runoff, leading to more frequent floods.
3.  **Changes in Land Use and Agriculture:** The shift from forests to agriculture and changes in cropping patterns can alter the surface albedo (reflectivity) and soil moisture, which can influence local rainfall patterns.
4.  **Aerosol Pollution:** High levels of aerosols (particulate matter) from industrial pollution and biomass burning can affect cloud formation processes. They can suppress light rainfall but can lead to more intense, heavy rainfall events when they do occur.
5.  **Impact of Large Dams and Reservoirs:** Large water bodies can increase local humidity and potentially influence local convection and rainfall patterns.
6.  **Groundwater Depletion:** Over-extraction of groundwater for irrigation can reduce soil moisture and evapotranspiration, potentially weakening the monsoon circulation in the long run.
7.  **Increased Frequency of Extreme Events:** While the total quantum of monsoon rainfall may not have changed significantly, human-induced climate change is leading to a higher frequency of extreme rainfall events (short, intense spells) and longer dry spells in between.
8.  **Weakening of Monsoon Circulation:** Some studies suggest that global warming, a result of human activities, may be weakening the overall land-sea temperature contrast that drives the monsoon.

**CONCLUSION:** The Indian monsoon is no longer a purely natural phenomenon. It is now a hybrid system where large-scale natural drivers are being increasingly modulated by human activities at the local and regional scale, making its behavior more erratic and extreme.

### Q13. Bring out the relationship between the shrinking Himalayan glaciers and the symptoms of climate change in the Indian sub-continent. 

**INTRO:** The shrinking of Himalayan glaciers is not just a local phenomenon but one of the most visible and alarming symptoms of climate change in the Indian subcontinent. This relationship is a direct cause-and-effect linkage, where rising global temperatures are driving glacial melt, which in turn is creating a cascade of environmental impacts.

**BODY:**

2.  **Symptom: Erratic Rainfall and Extreme Events:** Climate change is making rainfall patterns more erratic. This leads to less snowfall in winter (which nourishes glaciers) and more intense rainfall in summer, which accelerates melting and can trigger Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs).
3.  **Feedback Loop:** The retreat of glaciers exposes darker rock surfaces, which absorb more sunlight (lower albedo), further accelerating warming and melting in a dangerous feedback loop.
4.  **Consequence: Increased Frequency of GLOFs:** As glaciers melt, they form large, unstable glacial lakes. The bursting of these lakes, a direct consequence of warming, leads to devastating flash floods downstream. Example: The 2021 Chamoli disaster.
5.  **Consequence: Changes in River Flows:** The shrinking glaciers are altering the flow regimes of Himalayan rivers, leading to higher flows and flood risk in the short term, and reduced flows and water scarcity in the long term.
6.  **Consequence: Threat to Water Security:** This directly threatens the water security of millions in the Indian subcontinent who depend on these rivers, a clear impact of climate change.
7.  **Consequence: Impact on Biodiversity:** The changing cryosphere and river flows are impacting the unique biodiversity of the Himalayan region.

**CONCLUSION:** The shrinking Himalayan glaciers are the "canary in the coal mine" for climate change in the subcontinent. They are a stark and visible manifestation of the crisis, and their retreat is a direct threat to the water, food, and energy security of the entire region.

### Q14. Bring out the causes for the more frequent occurrence of landslides in the Himalayas than in the Western Ghats. 

**INTRO:** Both the Himalayas and the Western Ghats are prone to landslides, but the frequency and scale of landslides are significantly higher in the Himalayas. This is due to the fundamental differences in their geology, tectonic activity, and the intensity of human interference.

**BODY:**

1.  **Tectonic Activity:** The Himalayas are a young, tectonically active mountain range formed by the ongoing collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates. This makes the region highly seismic, and earthquakes are a major trigger for landslides. The Western Ghats, in contrast, are a stable, old block mountain on the passive continental margin.
2.  **Geology and Rock Structure:** The Himalayas are composed of relatively young, weak, and folded sedimentary rocks that are more prone to weathering and erosion. The Western Ghats are made of hard, crystalline basaltic rock, which is more resistant.
3.  **Steeper Slopes:** The Himalayas are much higher and have steeper and more unstable slopes compared to the more rounded and mature topography of the Western Ghats.
4.  **Glacial and Periglacial Processes:** The higher reaches of the Himalayas are subject to glacial erosion and freeze-thaw processes that shatter rocks and create a large amount of loose debris (scree), which is easily mobilized into landslides. These processes are absent in the Western Ghats.
5.  **Higher Intensity of Rainfall:** While the Western Ghats receive heavy rainfall, the Himalayas are prone to extremely high-intensity, short-duration rainfall events and cloudbursts, which have a greater capacity to trigger landslides.
6.  **Intense Human Interference:** The scale of infrastructure development, including road construction (e.g., Char Dham project), dam building, and urbanization, is much more intense and often unregulated in the Himalayas, leading to widespread slope destabilization.

**CONCLUSION:** The higher frequency of landslides in the Himalayas is a direct result of its dynamic geology and active tectonics, a situation that is being dangerously exacerbated by unplanned human development.

### Q15. There is no formation of deltas by rivers of the Western Ghats. Why ?

**INTRO:** The west-flowing rivers originating in the Western Ghats, such as the Narmada, Tapi, and the numerous short coastal rivers, do not form deltas at their mouths, unlike the east-flowing rivers like the Godavari and Krishna. This is due to a combination of factors related to the rivers' course, velocity, and the coastal geomorphology.

**BODY:**

1.  **Short and Swift Course:** These rivers have a much shorter course from their source in the Western Ghats to the Arabian Sea compared to the long course of the east-flowing rivers.
2.  **Steep Gradient:** They flow down a steeper gradient, which gives them a higher velocity. This high velocity does not allow for the deposition of sediments at the mouth.
3.  **Hard Rock Terrain:** They flow through hard, crystalline rocks of the Deccan Plateau, which are not easily eroded. As a result, they carry a much smaller load of silt and sediment.
4.  **Formation of Estuaries:** Instead of deltas, these rivers form estuaries, which are deep, narrow, funnel-shaped river mouths where the river current is strong and meets the sea tides.
5.  **Submerged Coastline:** The west coast of India is a submerged coastline, which is not conducive to delta formation. In contrast, the east coast is an emergent coastline, which is favorable for deposition.
6.  **Lack of Distributaries:** Due to their high velocity and confined channels, these rivers do not split into multiple distributaries, which is a key characteristic of a delta.



**CONCLUSION:** Therefore, the absence of deltas on the west coast is a direct consequence of the region's distinct geomorphology, characterized by short, fast-flowing rivers carrying a low sediment load over a hard rock terrain to a submerged coast.
