### Q1. Even after a good early start by Portuguese, they were not able to compete with their counterparts. Give reasons.
- *Intro:*
  - Despite early maritime success, the Portuguese failed to sustain power in India because they lacked resources, local alliances and strategic focus.
- *Body:*
  - Limited resources: Portugal had a small population and could not match the military and financial strength of Britain and the Dutch.
  - Coastal focus: Portuguese control remained limited to ports and forts, without deeper penetration into the Indian hinterland.
  - Naval setbacks: Losses to the British and Dutch weakened their Indian Ocean position and disrupted trade.
  - Poor diplomacy: They failed to build lasting alliances with major Indian powers like the Mughal court or Marathas.
  - Religious zeal: Forced conversions and persecution of Muslims created local hostility and resistance.
  - Global overreach: Brazil and African colonies diverted attention and investment away from India.
  - European competition: Stronger Dutch and British companies displaced them in key trade centres.
  - Administrative weakness: Their maritime empire lacked coherent governance and a stable revenue base.
- *Conclusion:*
  - The Portuguese could not compete because their empire was too small, too religiously rigid and too isolated against stronger European rivals.

### Q2. What are the impacts of Portuguese in India.
- *Intro:*
  - Portuguese rule left a lasting imprint on Indian politics, economy, society and culture through trade, religion and administration.
- *Body:*
  - Political Impact
    - Introduced the Viceroy system and directly controlled coastal enclaves such as Goa.
    - Annexed territory for economic advantage, shaping early European colonial governance.
  - Economic Impact
    - Initiated direct trade with Europe and reduced dependence on Arab middlemen.
    - Introduced the printing press, enabling wider dissemination of knowledge.
  - Cultural and Social Impact
    - Goa still reflects Portuguese culture in language, food and festivals.
    - Non-Christian communities faced persecution under missionary pressure.
    - Sati was condemned by missionaries and missionaries supported widow protection.
    - Introduced convent schools and Western education for Indians.
    - Promoted Bible translations in Kannada and Telugu to spread Christianity.
  - Agricultural Impact
    - Introduced New World crops like tobacco, potato, tomato, ladyfinger, guava and cashew.
    - Encouraged plantation agriculture along the western coast.
  - Architecture Impact
    - Introduced Iberian-style churches, forts and civic buildings, visible in Goan architecture.
    - Fortifications like Aguada and Bom Jesus became enduring colonial landmarks.
  - Naval Impact
    - Pioneered the blue-water policy in the Indian Ocean.
    - Practised gunboat diplomacy to control trade routes and coasts.
    - Demonstrated that naval power could determine colonial success.
    - Introduced multi-deck ships and European naval techniques.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Portuguese influence shaped Indian trade, religion and coastal culture, while introducing European institutions, new crops and early colonial administration.

### Q3. Significance of Vasco’s entry in India ?
- *Intro:*

- *Body:*
  - Maritime route: He established the Cape of Good Hope sea route to India, bypassing Arab and Ottoman-controlled land routes.

  - Diplomatic contact: It initiated direct relations between European powers and Indian rulers.
  - Economic impact: The voyage made spices and textiles more readily available in Europe.
  - Colonial precedent: It paved the way for later European expansion and competition in India.
  - Cultural encounter: European Christianity and new goods began to influence coastal India.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Vasco’s entry transformed India into a key theatre of European trade and imperial rivalry.

### Q5. Impact of French Rule in India ?
- *Intro:*
  - French rule in India left a distinct cultural and administrative legacy, especially along the Coromandel coast and in Pondicherry.
- *Body:*
  - Architecture Impact: 
    - Grid Layouts: French engineers split Puducherry into a grid-iron layout, separating the "White Town" (French) from "Black Town" (Indian).
    - pastel-colored colonial villas, high walls, wrought-iron balconies, and grid-iron street layouts reminiscent of a French Mediterranean town.
  - Culture and Language:
    - French: recognised official language in Pondicherry
    - Dual Nationality: The 1956 Treaty of Cession allowed local residents to choose French nationality, creating a unique community of thousands of French citizens living in India today.
  - Trade impact: French factories traded textiles, opium and spices with Southeast Asia and Europe.
  - Legal legacy: French civil law influenced administration in Pondicherry and other French enclaves.
  - Education: French schools and missionary institutions promoted Western learning.
  - Urban planning: Pondicherry developed as a planned colonial city with promenades, parks and civic buildings.
- *Conclusion:*
  - French rule was small-scale but left a lasting imprint in architecture, language, trade networks and colonial culture in South India.

### Q6. What were the causes of decline of Mughals post Aurangzeb ?
- *Intro:*
  - After Aurangzeb, the Mughal Empire weakened rapidly due to internal decay, regional revolts and external invasions.
- *Body:*
  - Religious intolerance: Aurangzeb’s policies alienated Hindu elites and provincial rulers.
  - Deccan ulcer: Prolonged southward campaigns drained the treasury and manpower.
  - Weak successors: Successive rulers lacked authority and military leadership.
  - Fiscal strain: Revenue demand fell as administration became corrupt and inefficient.
  - Provincial autonomy: Nawabs and zamindars became independent of Delhi.
  - Foreign invasions
    - 1739: Nadir Shah
    - 1761: Ahmed Shah Abdali
  - Rise of regional powers: Marathas, Sikhs, Rajputs and others challenged imperial authority.
  - Administrative decay: Central offices lost discipline and revenue collection collapsed.
- *Conclusion:*
  - The combination of religious intolerance, fiscal strain and powerful regional challengers caused the Mughal decline after Aurangzeb.


### Q7. How the rise of princely states took place?
- *Intro:*
  - The rise of princely states followed Mughal decline and the emergence of regional powers in the 18th century.
- *Body:*
  - Mughal fragmentation: The central authority collapsed, creating space for regional successors.
  - Successor states: Former Mughal provinces like Hyderabad, Carnatic, Bengal and Awadh became independent powers.
  - New regional powers: The Marathas, Sikhs, Afghans and Jats established strong territorial states.
  - Independent kingdoms: Mysore, Travancore, Kerala and Rajput states asserted autonomy.
  - Military leadership: Strong local rulers used their armies to defend and expand territory.
  - Revenue control: Local elites secured land revenue and financial independence from Delhi.
  - European interference: British and French support helped some princes consolidate their domains.
  - Political autonomy: Weak Mughal control allowed states to act as semi-sovereign entities.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Weak Mughal authority and active regional leaders led to the formation of princely states that later became the backbone of colonial indirect rule.

### Q8. How British conquest India ?
- *Intro:*
  - British conquest of India combined trade control, military power and political diplomacy to subdue regional rulers.
- *Body:*
  - Economic control: EIC gained revenue and market monopoly, as Bengal was fixed under the 1765 Diwani grant.
  - Military power: Superior artillery and disciplined sepoys defeated traditional armies, shown at Plassey and Buxar.
  - Political diplomacy: Subsidiary alliances and annexation isolated princes, as in Hyderabad and Awadh.
  - Administrative reform: Revenue systems and cantonments consolidated authority, seen in Bengal’s Permanent Settlement.
  - Naval dominance: Anglo-French rivalry was settled by British naval power, securing sea lanes and coastal trade.
  - Company finance: EIC profits funded armies and political agents, turning trade capital into territorial power.
  - Local collaboration: British used Indian intermediaries and rivalries to weaken larger states and gain allies.
  - Legal control: The Regulating Act and later charters expanded British legal oversight over Company actions.
  - Infrastructure base: Forts, factories and roads created a stable colonial framework for further expansion.
- *Conclusion:*
  - The conquest used commerce, force and statecraft to establish stable British colonial dominance over India.
### Q9. Role of Robert Clive in winning over India ?
- *Intro:*
  - Robert Clive laid the foundations of British power in India through military victories and savvy political alliances.
- *Body:*
  - Plassey victory: Clive’s win in 1757 over Siraj-ud-Daulah secured Bengal and shifted the balance in favour of the EIC.
  - Political intrigue: He exploited dissent among Bengali elites and installed Mir Jafar to create a pliant government.
  - Treaty diplomacy: Clive negotiated the Treaty of Allahabad (1765), gaining Diwani rights and legal supremacy in Bengal.
  - Revenue control: Diwani gave Britain fiscal dominance over Bengal, Bihar and Orissa, funding further expansion.
  - Military reforms: He reorganized the army with disciplined sepoys, European officers and better supply lines.
  - Textile monopoly: Clive strengthened EIC trade in Bengal goods, using revenue to undercut rivals and control markets.
  - Administrative precedent: He introduced the dual system of government that merged company power with local administration.
  - Strategic precedent: His actions set the pattern for British intervention in Indian politics through subsidiary alliances.
  - Legacy of authority: Clive’s victories made the EIC the de facto ruler in eastern India, enabling later territorial conquest.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Clive transformed the EIC from a trading company into a territorial power, paving the way for British dominance in India.
### Q10. What was the contribution of Raja Ram Mohan Roy towards eradicating social evils in Indian society ?
- *Intro:*
  - Raja Ram Mohan Roy pioneered social reform in India by challenging regressive customs through reason, law and education.
- *Body:*
  - Sati abolition: He campaigned against Sati and influenced the 1829 ban by Lord William Bentinck.
  - Widow rights: Roy argued for the humane treatment of widows and supported their right to remarry.
  - Religious reform: He founded the Brahmo Samaj to promote monotheism and oppose idolatry.
  - Education reform: He supported modern education, helped establish Hindu College, and promoted science and English learning.
  - Press freedom: Roy defended free expression so reformers could expose social injustice.
  - Caste critique: He criticised caste orthodoxy and urged a more rational social order.
  - Women’s uplift: He advocated women’s education and legal rights, challenging patriarchal customs.
  - Legal modernity: Roy used British law and appeals to the Governor-General to secure reform.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Roy’s reform leadership weakened key social evils and created the intellectual basis for modern Indian social change.
### Q11. Contribution of Dadabhai Naroji.
- *Intro:*
  - Dadabhai Naroji exposed colonial exploitation and laid the foundation of Indian political nationalism.
- *Body:*
  - Drain theory: He argued that British rule drained Indian wealth to Britain, worsening Indian poverty.
  - Indian question: Naroji took Indian grievances to the British Parliament as the first Indian MP.
  - Economic analysis: He used statistics to show low Indian incomes and unfair taxation.
  - Political mobilisation: He helped found the Indian National Congress in 1885.
  - Social reform: He campaigned for education, social uplift and political rights for Indians.
  - Moral critique: His writings challenged British claims of benevolent rule.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Naroji made Indian economic suffering visible internationally, boosting the demand for self-rule.

### Q16. What were smart policies that British adopted for annexation besides war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The British used diplomacy, legal tools and financial pressure to annex Indian states without open warfare.
- *Body:*
  - Subsidiary alliances: Dependent states lost sovereignty in exchange for protection.
  - Doctrine of lapse: States without heirs were annexed automatically.
  - Mercenary policy: British troops were stationed in princely courts to influence rulers.
  - Treaty manipulation: Unequal treaties limited rulers and expanded British control.
  - Financial dependency: Debt and subsidy obligations made states pliant.
  - Political courts: Residents monitored and guided native governments.
  - Legal pretexts: British advisers invoked misgovernment to justify annexation.
- *Conclusion:*
  - These policies allowed Britain to enlarge its domain by exploiting weakness, dependency and legal loopholes.
### Q17. What were the causes of 1857 war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The 1857 war was triggered by military, political and cultural grievances against British Company rule.
- *Body:*
  - Sepoy discontent: Low pay, poor terms and unequal treatment angered Indian soldiers.
  - Cartridge issue: The greased cartridge controversy gave a religious spark to existing resentment.
  - Annexation policy: Doctrine of lapse and annexation of Oudh threatened princely rulers.
  - Land revenue burden: Peasants suffered under heavy taxes and revenue settlements.
  - Religious interference: Actions perceived as attacks on Hindu and Muslim customs created fear.
  - Economic hardship: Destruction of Indian industry and rural distress fuelled anger.
  - Political exclusion: Indians had no voice in administration, making rebellion more likely.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Combined military, social and economic grievances made 1857 a broad-based uprising against British rule.
### Q18. What was the impact of 1857 war ?
- *Intro:*
  - The 1857 war ended Company rule and led to major political and military restructuring in India.
- *Body:*
  - End of EIC: Power passed to the British Crown under the 1858 Government of India Act.
  - Army reorganisation: British troops increased and Indian soldiers were reorganised to prevent mutiny.
  - Policy change: The Crown adopted a policy of non-interference in religion and customs.
  - Princely states: Many rulers were restored and treated as allies rather than annexed.
  - Administrative caution: The British became more conservative in reform and expansion.
  - Racial divide: British mistrust of Indians grew, increasing racial segregation.
  - National awakening: The uprising became a symbol for later nationalist movements.
- *Conclusion:*
  - The rebellion reshaped colonial rule, ending Company domination and making British governance more direct and controlled.
### Q19. What were the revolutionary activities before 1857 war ?
- *Intro:*
  - Before 1857, Indian resistance took the form of local mutinies, tribal uprisings and military unrest.
- *Body:*
  - Vellore Mutiny: In 1806, Indian soldiers rebelled against dress and religious interference.
  - Paika Rebellion: In 1817, the Paikas of Odisha rose against revenue and land policies.
  - Santhal revolt: In 1855, tribal cultivators revolted against zamindars and British oppression.
  - Tribal resistance: Munda, Koya and other groups fought against land loss and exploitation.
  - Civilian unrest: Peasant protests reflected widespread rural distress under colonial rule.
  - Military discontent: Local army mutinies showed that soldier grievances existed before 1857.
  - Precedent: These uprisings prepared the ground for the larger rebellion of 1857.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Early uprisings exposed colonial weaknesses and helped shape the broader revolt of 1857.
### Q20. Enumerate Lytton's aggressive policy and Rippon's to settle down people's aggression in India.
- *Intro:*
  - Lytton used repression while Ripon responded with measured reforms to ease Indian unrest.
- *Body:*
  - Vernacular Press Act: Lytton curbed nationalist newspapers in 1878.
  - Ilbert Bill crisis: Lytton’s attempt to allow Indian judges over Europeans caused elite backlash.
  - Land and tax policy: Lytton followed a hard line during famine and agrarian distress.
  - Public works: He invested in railways and irrigation but ignored political demands.
  - Ripon’s local self-government: He introduced elected municipal boards in 1882.
  - Education reform: Ripon expanded primary education and supported universities.
  - Judicial and police reform: Ripon relaxed press restrictions and improved civil liberties.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Lytton’s aggression provoked political resistance, while Ripon’s reforms aimed to placate Indians and build trust.

- *Intro:*
  - British conquest succeeded due to military advantage, economic control and skilful political manipulation.
- *Body:*
  - Naval dominance: Control of the seas isolated Indian rivals and protected British trade.

### Q22. What were Administrative Changes During The British Conquest & The Role Of Governor Generals
- *Intro:*
  - Governor-Generals restructured administration to consolidate colonial rule through law, revenue and bureaucracy.
- *Body:*
  - Cornwallis reforms: Introduced civil service exams and separated revenue, judicial and executive functions.
  - Wellesley centralisation: Expanded political control through Residents and subsidiary alliances.
  - Dalhousie modernisation: Built railways, telegraphs and postal networks to integrate the colony.
  - Revenue systems: Permanent Settlement, Ryotwari and Mahalwari shaped land administration.
  - Police and judicial reform: British institutions established law and order in newly acquired territories.
  - Financial control: Governor-Generals supervised company budgets and fiscal policy.
  - Executive authority: They combined military, civil and political power at the top of colonial administration.
- *Conclusion:*
  - Governor-Generals transformed India’s administration into an efficient colonial state that served British interests.
